Chapter 1

The Weight of Wheels

The wind carried the scent of plant oil and distant storm as Veera adjusted the cargo straps one final time.

Dawn was breaking copper and thin across the eastern plain, the light catching on scrub-grass still wet with dew and on the rusted skeletons of drilling platforms that had stood dead longer than anyone alive could remember. The air was cool but carried the promise of heat, the kind that would press itself against the earth by midday and turn the horizon to liquid.

The staging ground lay at the settlement’s eastern edge, a broad apron of packed earth between the last row of sleeping-houses and the open plain, and at this hour it was the only awake place in the world. Loading ramps of rough timber stood in a line where the barrels came down on rollers from the oil-sheds, and the night-lamps still burned along the shed wall, going pale as the sky took over their work. Breath steamed. Chains ticked as they warmed. Somewhere behind the sheds the settlement was beginning its own day, but a rope strung on posts marked where that day ended and the convoy’s began, and the two did not mix.

Six barrels of tlalli-tetl, sacred earth-blood, secured to a frame that towered above her like a mechanical buffalo. Each barrel worth more than most people earned in a season, and all of it balanced on wheels that sang when they turned, a low harmonic hum that rose from the spokes like something remembered rather than made.

The transport was the largest machine the settlement owned that did not stand still. Nose to tail it ran the length of two ordinary bikes, a spine of heavy-gauge steel tubing reinforced at every joint where load met frame, and its cargo bed rode behind the saddle on struts thick as a woman’s wrist. The wheels stood as high as Veera’s ribs, wide-rimmed, shod in composite tires of cured resin and woven cloth that gave against stone instead of shattering, and the gearing below the bed was cut low and patient, made to move mass, not to hurry it. Behind the saddle rose the mast, socketed into the frame like a ship’s, and from the mast hung the boom that would carry the sail. A rider did not so much mount the transport as enter into negotiations with it. Loaded, it outweighed her eight times over, and it would answer her exactly as well as she understood it, and no better.

“Your heart beats too loud, Wind Rider.”

Supervisor Maddox approached from the staging area where the other bikes were being checked and loaded. The chrome monitor on his wrist, a palm-sized disc fused to the skin, pulsed once as he neared her. Reading her. Her heart rate, her oxygen saturation, the output of the lungs the gene-readers had given her. He glanced at the disc and seemed satisfied. He used her childhood name mockingly, a name that belonged to someone her grandmother had believed she could become.

“Load secured, Supervisor,” Veera said. She tested her rudder lever, a steel arm mounted at the hip that controlled the angle of the sail boom, and felt the system respond with a faint vibration through the frame. The great triangle of grass-cloth hung furled above her, pale green and smelling of the prairie where it had been woven. When unfurled, it could catch wind and transform pedaling into something approaching flight.

“Remember your place,” Maddox said, leaning close enough that she caught the synthetic oil scent marking his elevated genetics. “You’re cargo transport. The Honor Society doesn’t trade with Workers directly.”

His own bike gleamed beside him. Silk sail dyed supervisor-blue, brass fittings that would never see real work. Pretty. Useless in a fight.

Veera said nothing. She let her fingers drift to the cord beneath her collar and traced the shape hidden there: a pendant no larger than her thumb, carved from horse bone gone yellow with age, its surface worn smooth by her grandmother’s hands and then by her own. A tiny skull with prayers cut into the brow in a script the gene-readers had never catalogued. Contraband in a world that discouraged remembering. She wore it against her sternum where the broad plate of her chest kept it pressed flat and invisible beneath the rough-spun fabric of her Worker’s shirt. The pendant was the only thing against her skin that she had chosen to carry. Everything else, the wide ribcage, the deepened lungs, the faint pale streaks that ran across her shoulders and collarbones like veins drawn to the surface, had been chosen for her.

Around her, the convoy assembled in the gray light. Three Supervisors on their light, gleaming bikes, checking monitors and consulting manifests. Eight Workers like Veera, broad-chested and heavy-framed, tightening loads on transport frames built for burden. And one Scout, Jana, already mounted at the convoy’s forward edge, her bike stripped lean for speed, her eyes scanning the plain with the particular focus that her genetic line was known for.

At the rope, a handful of settlement children had gathered to watch the loading. Most of them watched the barrels. One girl stood apart with her head tipped to the side, eyes nowhere, listening to the near wheel as the load settled. Then a shed-hand called something, and the children scattered like sparrows.

“Mount up!” Maddox called.

Veera swung over the transport’s modified saddle, a broad leather seat reinforced with steel plate, and settled her weight into the frame. Her body knew this posture. Shoulders forward, chest open to let the deepened lungs fill, legs finding the pedal stroke that could be sustained for hours without her heart rate climbing above what the monitors considered acceptable. The massive wheels began to turn beneath her, thick-rimmed and heavy, built to absorb the ruts and stones of unpaved prairie. As the southern wind built across the open ground, she reached up and released the sail. The grass-cloth unfurled with a snap and caught air with a sound like laughter, and the grinding work of hauling became something else entirely: rider, machine, and wind all moving as one.

The sail was not a passenger. It worked, and it demanded work. Through the rudder lever her left hand held a running argument with the wind, feeling each gust arrive in the boom a heartbeat before it arrived in her spokes, feeding it into the frame at the angle that turned sideways push into forward pull. Her grandmother had called the lever the reins. Ten years of hauling had taught Veera why: hold it too tight and the wind fought you, hold it too loose and the wind left you, and somewhere between was the line where the great sail leaned into its harness and pulled.

The convoy spread across the Plain of Memories under a sky that stretched without limit in every direction. The land here was flat and wind-scoured, tufted with brown grass that bent in waves like the surface of an inland sea. They followed paths worn so deep into the earth that no amount of wind or weather had been able to fill them. Old paths. Older than anyone Veera had known could account for. The ruts ran two abreast the whole way, wide-set, cut to a gauge no bicycle matched, and in places the stone itself was worn glossy, as though something heavy and patient had passed that way a hundred thousand times before the first wheel Veera would recognize was ever forged.

Twisted metal formations rose from the grass at intervals, some leaning, some collapsed entirely, their steel frames gone the color of dried blood. Oil-ravens. Veera had ridden past them her entire working life, these drilling platforms that had punched through bedrock in search of something the world no longer permitted anyone to name aloud. Up close, you could see where the earth had buckled around their bases, the ground cracked and pushed upward as though something beneath had tried to spit them out. Nothing grew inside the buckled rings. Even the brown grass kept its distance, so that each fallen platform lay in its own circle of bare white earth, like a body outlined where it fell.

Her grandmother had told her what they were. What they had done. How the drilling had gone too deep and the earth had answered by folding in on itself, crushing coastlines together, swallowing the distances between continents until the map of the world became a single wounded landmass. The Great Convergence. It was history that lived in the landscape itself, and you learned it not from books but from riding through the wreckage it left behind.

Her grandmother had not been the only one who taught that way. I am not the last who keeps, she had said once, at the hearth, in the voice she used for things that went no further than the fire. I am only the one who keeps you. Veera had been a child then, and had thought it was comfort. She had been years understanding it was arithmetic.

But the earth also remembered older patterns. Wind-paths. Star-roads. The ways that connected all living things. The routes the convoy followed were proof of that. Older than oil, older than drilling, older than any technology that had tried to replace what the land already knew.

For two hours they rode in good formation, sails catching steady southern wind. The Workers settled into their hauling rhythm, the deep steady cadence of effort that came as naturally to them as breathing. Veera could hear the Worker beside her, a man named Pol, his lungs pulling air in the same measured pattern as hers. Where his collar had loosened in the wind, she could see the streaks across his neck, the same pale lines she carried, running just beneath the skin. On Pol they were darker, more pronounced. The gene-readers would have called that a marker of superior oxygen transport. Pol would have called it nothing. It was just what they were.

But beneath it, Veera felt the other rhythm, the one her grandmother had taught her to listen for. The conversation between wind and wheel and the body that connected them. The way a shift in breeze could be answered with a shift in weight, the way the bike responded not to force but to partnership. She let her hands soften on the grips and felt the transport settle into a line that required less effort, the frame finding its own efficiency the way water finds the lowest path.

The sun climbed and the cool air burned off and the plain opened before them in shades of gold and rust. They passed a cluster of oil-ravens so corroded that the wind played through their hollow frames, producing a low moaning sound that Veera’s grandmother had called the earth grieving. Beyond them, the grass grew taller, untouched by the salt that poisoned the ground closer to the old drill sites.

Then Scout Jana raised her hand from the front of the column.

Danger approaching.

* * *

Veera’s enhanced lungs caught scents the others missed. Sharpened metal, hunting oils, the musk of predators. But more telling was how the wind eddied around obstacles that shouldn’t exist, deflecting off shapes too tall and too narrow to be anything natural on this flat ground.

“Stilts sign,” Jana called back, voice tight.

Fresh tracks in the hard earth. Too narrow for wheels, too regular for animals. The distinctive pattern of the Stone-Walkers, nomads who had learned to live above the salt-poisoned ground on platforms of welded steel, hunting from heights where eagles feared to fly.

“Scavenger tricks,” Maddox muttered, but his hand found the handle of his obsidian blade, a short curved weapon that hung at his hip in a leather sheath.

Veera studied the tracks with her grandmother’s training. The puncture marks were deep and evenly spaced, the gait of machines moving with purpose. These weren’t random wanderings. The Stone-Walkers were approaching from the ritual direction of conflict. This was planned.

The other Workers felt it too. She could see it in the way they hunched lower over their handlebars, the way their breathing changed, the streaks across their shoulders seeming to stand out sharper against skin gone pale with fear.

“Supervisor, the pattern suggests.”

“Workers don’t read sign. Pedal, don’t think.”

The plain went quiet. Even the wind seemed to hesitate, dropping for a long moment so that the only sound was the creak of cargo straps and the hum of wheels on packed earth. The silence had a weight to it, a held-breath quality that Veera had felt once before when a dust storm was building on the southern edge and the sky couldn’t decide whether to open or hold.

Then the war-cries came, and the earth trembled.

They materialized from the shimmer of heat that hung above the plain in the late morning, shapes resolving out of distortion like giants assembling themselves from air and fury. Towering figures on razor stilts, their walking-houses swaying high above the ground like temple towers come to life, each platform a mobile fortress of welded scrap and cured hide. The stilts themselves were extraordinary: triple-jointed legs of salvaged steel, each ending in a sharpened point that bit into earth with mechanical precision. The ground carried their coming before the eye could hold them steady, each stride landing with a driven-stake thud that Veera felt in her own frame, in her wheel-rims, in her teeth, a drumbeat walking.

The weapons brought ice to Veera’s veins. Harpoons longer than a man, mounted on pivot systems that let the warriors aim downward at ground prey the way a hawk tracks a mouse. The bone tips had been treated with something that caught the sunlight and threw it back in sick yellow flashes.

The lead Walker descended toward them like a striking eagle, his platform decorated with victory banners that snapped in the wind of his movement, his war-song rising in a language Veera didn’t know but understood in her chest. The harpoon he drew back gleamed with wicked purpose.

“SCATTER! ABANDON CARGO!”

The convoy exploded apart. Supervisors peeled away first, Maddox among them, his blue sail catching wind as he banked hard to the south without looking back. The other Workers released their cargo straps with the practiced desperation of people who had drilled this scenario since childhood: drop the load, drop the weight, pedal until your engineered heart threatens to burst. Pol’s barrels hit the ground and split, sending dark oil pooling into the dust. Another Worker went down when a harpoon struck the earth a meter from her front wheel and the impact threw her sideways. She scrambled up and ran for her bike with a sound that was not quite a scream.

Jana had already vanished toward the eastern horizon, doing what Scouts were meant to do: survive to report.

But Veera was trapped. Six barrels of precious oil, months of labor and prayers that the settlement couldn’t afford to lose. The transport’s mass held her like an anchor while death descended on stilts above her. Her hands were shaking on the grips. Her engineered heart was doing exactly what it had been built to do, pumping blood at volume, flooding her muscles with oxygen, preparing her body for sustained flight. But flight was not what her grandmother’s voice was telling her.

As the Stone-Walker’s harpoon descended, that voice cut through terror: The Sacred Horses were not the largest beasts, granddaughter. But they had riders who understood that courage is the decision to dance with death when the People need you to dance.

The transport wasn’t built for war. But as Veera looked at it with newly clear eyes, she saw not cargo-hauler but war-horse. Massive frame. Powerful wheels. Sail system that could catch wind like a lance catching light.

She yanked the rudder lever hard to starboard while throwing her weight into the pedals. The sail caught wind and drove the massive bike into a spiraling charge that carried all the fury of her ancestors’ cavalry strikes. Eight times her own weight came around with her, the load leaning out against the straps, the frame groaning at work it had never been asked to do, and the wheels’ hum climbed through their long even note into something higher and stranger, a singing Veera had never heard from them, as though the machine had been waiting its whole working life to find out what it was for.

The harpoon’s point scraped steel with a sound like earth screaming, but Veera had already shifted trajectory. The Stone-Walker, committed to his strike, couldn’t adjust as she drove metal and cargo directly into his forward stilts.

Impact rang across the plain like a war-drum struck by gods. The Walker’s legs buckled, sending him tumbling earthward in a cascade of twisted metal. His war-house tilted dangerously, but even falling, he moved with warrior grace, using his remaining stilts to control his descent.

Veera rolled clear, coming up with tire-iron raised. Her stance was pure warrior-dancer, balanced and ready, her grandmother’s training overriding years of conditioning. Around them, other Stone-Walkers had paused their pursuit, war-songs shifting from hunting-calls to something like approval.

* * *

The fallen Walker emerged from his damaged stilts with movements that spoke of countless battles. He was tall even without the mechanical legs, broad across the shoulders, and he moved with the economy of someone who had long ago stopped wasting motion on anything unnecessary. When he removed his war-mask, the face beneath was weathered and painted in geometric patterns of ochre and ash, and his eyes were sharp as obsidian blades.

His voice, when he spoke, carried the musical accent of wind-singers.

“You fight with the heart of old blood, yet wear the marks of the cargo-bound.” His gaze moved to the streaks visible at her collar. “This is a riddle worth solving.”

“I protect what I carry,” Veera replied, though the words felt thin against the truth awakening in her chest.

He laughed, a sound like wind-chimes of polished bone. “What you carry, yes. But also what carries you.” He gestured to his damaged stilts with fondness, the way a rider might touch an injured horse. “These legs were forged from oil-raven steel, tempered with prayers. But without a rider who understands their spirit, they are only metal reaching toward sky.”

He studied the wreckage of his forward stilts, then looked back at Veera’s transport, its frame bent from the impact but still standing.

“You made your cargo-beast into a war-horse. This speaks of something the gene-readers cannot account for.”

“Blood-memory?” she asked. The word rose from somewhere deeper than her education.

“Knowledge that lives in bone and sinew, passed down from when the People rode Sacred Horses across endless plains.” He said it simply, as though stating a fact about weather or terrain. “Gene-readers test heart-strength and lung-capacity, but they cannot measure deeper inheritances.” His painted face held something between amusement and reverence. “Cannot breed out grandmother-dreams.”

The wind picked up again across the plain, carrying dust and the smell of heated metal. Around them, the other Stone-Walkers waited on their platforms with the patience of hunters who had seen this kind of exchange before. Veera was aware of the scattered convoy in the distance, tiny figures on bikes watching from what they hoped was a safe remove.

The Walker signaled his companions: tactical withdrawal, not defeat. Before departing, he reached into a pouch at his belt and dropped something at her feet. A carved horse, small enough to close a fist around, its mane intertwined with bicycle wheels in patterns that spoke of prophecy and possibility.

“I am Tlacaelel of the Stone-Walkers, Speaker-of-High-Places, Keeper-of-Rising-Paths. You who fight with horse-heart reborn, what name do you carry?”

“Veera.”

“Veera-Who-Rides-Steel-Horses,” he amended, investing her name with warrior-weight. “We came seeking oil, but found something more valuable. Proof that old blood runs true in gene-bred generations.”

He paused, studying her with eyes that saw beyond caste markers. The sun had climbed high enough now that the heat-shimmer was building again on the plain, making the distant landscape waver.

“Do you know why we walk so high above earth?”

She shook her head.

“When the Great Convergence broke the world, our ancestors found themselves on lands thrust up from ocean floors. Soil poisoned with salt and deep-place toxins, useless for growing oil-plants. So our wise ones chose: if earth could not feed us, we would live above it. We built steel legs and wind-houses, learned to hunt from high places as eagles do.”

He turned and looked north, where the plain stretched flat and featureless into haze. The gesture was deliberate. He wanted her to look.

“But we are also Keepers-of-High-Perspective, who see patterns hidden from ground-travelers. From our heights, we have observed troubling signs.” His painted face darkened. “Movement in the northern reaches where old oil-fields lie buried. Machines that walk but do not breathe, led by those who have forgotten that earth is more than resource to consume.”

He left the threat unfinished. The implications were clear enough. The delicate balance of bicycle societies, wind-trade routes, careful plant-oil husbandry, all of it could be swept away if someone tapped the old power sources.

“Why tell me this? I’m just a Worker.”

“Supervisors see only efficiency calculations. You have blood-memory. You can read patterns that connect ancient ways to necessary futures.” He turned toward his waiting companions, who were already adjusting their stilts for travel. “When the time comes to choose between easy paths and right paths, it will be riders like you who make the difference.”

The Stone-Walkers departed as the sun passed its highest point, tall figures receding into the shimmer of heat until they looked like the drilling platforms themselves, just more strange shapes standing impossibly above the plain. They left questions burning in Veera’s chest and a carved horse that seemed to hold the weight of prophecy.

* * *

The convoy regrouped slowly, shame and relief mixing on familiar faces. Supervisor Maddox returned last, silk sail torn, chrome monitor cracked.

“How?” he demanded, staring at the wreckage. “Workers aren’t trained for combat.”

“I remembered something my grandmother taught me.”

“Workers aren’t supposed to remember. They follow procedure.”

Veera turned the carved horse in her hands, studying where mane became spokes, hooves transformed to pedals. “Perhaps procedure isn’t the only path to efficiency.”

They completed the journey in silence. The Workers rode a half-length wider around her transport than they had at dawn, and twice she caught Pol watching her hands instead of the road. But Veera’s mind worked at the patterns Tlacaelel had described, turning them over the way her fingers turned the carved horse. As they crested the final ridge and saw Honor Society banners flying from their fortress-compound, she made a decision that would have been unthinkable at dawn.

She would deliver the oil. She would maintain the compliance facades that kept Workers alive. But she would also observe everything with eyes newly opened to patterns connecting present to past, individual to tribe, rider to mount.

The Honor Society’s compound spread before them. From the ridge the two rings showed whole, an outer wall and an inner one drawn like a wheel within a wheel, forge-smoke rising from the hub in three steady columns. Circular earthworks following sacred geometries. Workshops where the finest bicycles were crafted. Testing grounds where riders proved worthiness. Ceremonial halls where passage-rites bonded rider to machine with religious precision.

Her damaged transport groaned toward the gates, but Veera felt stronger than ever. The weight that had once defined her now felt like preparation for journeys beyond predetermined paths. The carved horse rested warm in her palm, its wooden curves holding the memory of Sacred Horses running free across boundless plains.

The compound gates opened. Veera guided her bike through the entrance, carrying something no breeding program had anticipated and no gene-reader could measure.

Somewhere in the distance, Stone-Walkers continued their eternal patrol, watching from high places for the signs Tlacaelel had spoken of. Movement in northern reaches where old hungers stirred, and petroleum dragons dreamed of waking once more.

Chapter 2

The Forge of Honor

The Honor Society’s gates stood three times the height of a loaded transport, two leaves of gray timber banded in black iron, hung on hinges thicker than Veera’s thigh. Their outward faces carried no handle, no knocker, no seam wide enough for a blade. Whoever wanted in stood on the road beneath the watch-ledge and waited to be seen, and the gates opened from within, or they did not open at all. The Honor Society’s gates bore the scars of a hundred raids. Gouges from harpoons, dents from ramming attacks, burns from fire-arrows that had tried and failed to breach their defenses. Every mark had been left where it landed, unfilled and unpainted. A gouge from some old harpoon still held the clean shape of its point. The gates were a ledger of what had been attempted and what it had earned.

But what struck Veera most was the sound: rhythmic hammering of metal on metal echoing from within like a giant’s heartbeat, punctuated by the hiss of steam and the ring of perfectly tuned bicycle chains under tension. The compound was not waiting quietly behind its walls. It was working. She had the sudden sense of arriving at the body of some great animal and hearing, through its hide, the labor of its heart.

“State your business,” called the gatekeeper, a woman whose arms bore the ritual scars of the master crafters. She stood on a watch-ledge built out over the gate’s crossbeam, a shelf of planking behind a chest-high rail, reached by an iron stair that switchbacked up the inside of the wall, and from that height she could see the whole road and everyone on it. Her forearms rested bare on the rail despite the cooling afternoon, and even at that distance the scars showed: pale lines laid in matched pairs from wrist to elbow, deliberate as script, too regular to be the accidents of labor. Marks that had been given, and accepted, and worn since.

Racked in an open alcove in the wall’s thickness beside the gate, her bike stood in plain view of the road, and Veera forgot her manners looking at it. Her bike was a work of art. Frame geometry that followed mathematical principles Veera had never seen, wheels that seemed to barely touch the ground, a sail system so refined it could catch wind from a whisper. It was not decorated. It did not need to be.

“Cargo delivery,” Supervisor Maddox replied, his damaged monitor catching the afternoon light. “Six barrels of grade-one tlalli-tetl for the Forgemasters.”

The gatekeeper’s eyes moved past him to Veera’s damaged transport, taking in the bent frame, the improvised repairs, the way the massive bike listed to one side. The great machine told its own story to anyone who knew how to read one: a frame built twice the length of a standard bike, its cargo bed towering with the strapped barrels, its splinted forks holding the whole of it at a wounded angle, like a beast favoring one leg. Instead of dismissal, Veera saw something unexpected: recognition.

“You fought today,” the gatekeeper said. It wasn’t a question.

“Stone-Walkers,” Maddox answered quickly, his voice pitching higher at the attention sliding past him. “Worker got lucky.”

“Luck.” The gatekeeper came down the iron stair without hurry, and up close her voice carried the dry humor of a woman who knew what it was to test her skills against death, and had lived to speak of it. “Is that what we call tactical innovation now?” She hauled on a counterweighted lever set into the wall, and the nearer gate-leaf swung inward on its huge hinges with a sound like a mountain clearing its throat. Through the widening gap, the compound showed itself in pieces: stone, smoke, motion. “Forgemaster Kessa will want to see this.”

* * *

The compound was a maze of workshops, training grounds, and ceremonial spaces that followed patterns older than the gene-readers’ settlements. The buildings were stone below and timber above, most of them two stories, their roofs vented with louvers that breathed thin columns of forge-heat into the reddening sky, and the light was low and long by now, catching the smoke and turning it to bronze. Everywhere Veera looked, she saw the marriage of ancient wisdom and mechanical innovation. Apprentices barely old enough to reach the pedals worked alongside masters whose hands moved with the precision of surgeons, their tools singing as they shaped metal into forms that seemed to capture wind itself.

“Look but don’t touch,” Maddox warned under his breath. “These people value their secrets more than their lives.”

They passed a testing ground where young riders put experimental bikes through trials that would have terrified even experienced cyclists. It was an oval of packed earth sunk slightly below the surrounding paths and ringed by a berm of piled stone, and the riders’ shadows ran long across it in the late light, the dust they raised hanging gold above the course. Jumps requiring perfect timing. Obstacle courses demanding split-second decisions. Speed trials where a single mistake meant catastrophic failure. But what fascinated Veera was the way each rider seemed to move in perfect harmony with their machine. Not controlling it. Dancing with it.

Her hands tightened on the transport’s handlebars. Something in her chest responded to that harmony, a pull she couldn’t name, as though her body recognized what it was seeing before her mind could catch up.

“The Forgemaster’s workshop,” the gatekeeper announced, stopping before a building that thrummed with controlled power. It was the tallest building Veera had yet seen inside the walls, a full three stories of fitted stone, windowless on the ground floor and rising to a peaked timber roof crowned with a chimney-stack that fumed steadily against the sky. She could feel the thrumming through the soles of her boots from ten paces out, and the double doors at its face stood as tall as the compound gates in miniature, their surfaces darkened by generations of smoke and hands. “Leave your transport here. Workers may observe but not participate.”

* * *

Inside, the workshop was a cathedral of mechanical precision. The doors opened onto a raised threshold, and the working floor lay two broad steps below it, so that anyone entering stood for a moment looking down over the whole room the way the gatekeeper had looked down over the road. Bicycle frames hung from the rafters like steel prayers, their geometry speaking of aerodynamics and stress distribution that pushed the boundaries of what metal could achieve. High clerestory windows let down shafts of the day’s last light, and in them the forge-dust drifted like slow sparks. Forgemaster Kessa stood at the center of it all, a woman whose graying hair was bound with wire and whose hands bore burns from a lifetime of shaping steel. Her place was a broad scarred workbench on the sunken floor, and everything else in the room seemed arranged in rings around it.

Kessa looked up as they entered, her eyes immediately focusing on Veera. “So,” she said, her voice carrying up the steps with the authority of someone accustomed to being obeyed, “you’re the one who turned a cargo hauler into a cavalry charge.”

“Forgemaster,” Maddox began, hurrying down the steps, “the Worker simply followed her panic instincts.”

“Supervisors speak when spoken to.” Kessa had not taken her eyes from Veera, and her next words arrived as though Maddox had made no sound at all. “I’m addressing the rider who improvised a ram-charge against aerial opponents. That takes more than panic. It takes understanding.”

The gatekeeper had wheeled the transport just inside the doors, and Kessa crossed to it. She walked around Veera’s damaged transport like a healer examining a wounded patient. Her hands traced the bent frame, tested the sail rigging, examined the impact points where steel had met steel. “Interesting,” she murmured. “The frame geometry is all wrong for combat maneuvering, but you compensated with sail-work and momentum transfer. Crude, but effective.”

“The oil delivery,” Maddox tried again.

“The oil will be processed in due time.” Kessa fixed Veera with a stare that seemed to see through flesh to the bones beneath. “Well? Don’t let your Supervisor speak for you. What happened out there?”

The carved horse in her pocket pulsed with warmth. Tlacaelel’s words echoed: when the time comes to choose between easy paths and right paths.

“I stopped seeing the bike as a cargo hauler,” she said, her voice steady despite the risk. “Started seeing it as something that could fight.”

“And what gave you that insight?”

“My grandmother’s stories. About the Sacred Horses and the riders who understood them.”

The workshop fell silent except for distant hammering. Maddox’s face had gone pale. Workers weren’t supposed to reference the old ways, weren’t supposed to think beyond their assigned functions. But Kessa smiled, and the expression transformed her stern features entirely.

“Your grandmother was wise.” She moved to a workbench where pieces of a bicycle lay disassembled like the components of a complex prayer. Her hands began putting them together with movements too quick to follow. “Tell me, Worker. What is your name?”

“Veera.”

“Veera,” Kessa repeated, testing the sound. “In the old tongue, that means brave one.” The bike was taking shape under her hands. Not the bulky frame of a cargo hauler, but something sleeker, more responsive. “The Honor Society has a tradition. When we encounter someone who shows true understanding of the rider-mount bond, we offer them a choice.”

“Forgemaster,” Maddox interrupted, his voice tight with alarm, “this is a Worker. She has assigned duties, genetic limitations.”

“She has potential. And potential transcends genetic categories.” Kessa continued working, the bike’s frame now clearly visible, lighter than Veera’s transport but stronger, designed for speed and maneuverability rather than cargo. “The choice is this: return to your settlement with your cargo delivered and your duties fulfilled. Or stay for three days and participate in our Trial of Understanding.”

“What kind of trial?”

“You would be given a basic frame and access to our forges. Three days to build a bike that reflects your understanding of the partnership between rider and mount. If you succeed, you earn the right to train with us regardless of your birth-caste. If you fail...” She shrugged. “You return to cargo hauling with the knowledge that you tried.”

The offer hung in the air, heavy with possibility and danger. Workers didn’t participate in Honor Society trials. The genetic hierarchies made such things impossible, except that Kessa was standing here making them possible.

* * *

“This is irregular,” Maddox protested. “The trade agreements don’t include provisions for Worker training. My supervisors will demand explanations.”

“Your supervisors can demand whatever they like.” Kessa’s calm carried the confidence of someone who commanded respect through skill rather than genetic advantage. “The Honor Society honors courage over chromosomes. If they have complaints, they can bring them to me personally.”

Veera looked at the bike taking shape under Kessa’s hands. The curve of a handlebar that echoed something older than steel. The balance point where a rider’s weight would settle into partnership with the frame. Her fingers itched to touch it, and the itching felt like recognition.

“If I stayed,” she said carefully, “if I attempted this trial, what would I be building? What kind of bike?”

Kessa’s expression shifted from cool appraisal to the satisfaction of a teacher whose student had asked the right question. “That would be entirely up to you. The trial isn’t about following specifications. It’s about translating your understanding into physical form.” She gestured toward the workshop around them. “Every bike we forge here is unique because every rider is unique. Your creation would reflect your spirit, your needs, your vision of what the partnership should be.”

“Three days,” Veera said. It wasn’t really a question.

“Three days to prove that understanding transcends engineering.”

She touched the carved horse in her pocket, feeling its smooth curves beneath her fingers. Tlacaelel had called it blood-memory awakening. Here was a chance to find out if he was right, to discover whether the dreams of grandmothers could awaken in the hands of their descendants.

Accepting would mean defying everything she had been taught about her place in the world. It would mean choosing the hard path over the easy one, exactly as the Stone-Walker had predicted.

“I’ll do it,” she said. The words came out stronger than she felt. “I accept the trial.”

Kessa nodded as if she had never doubted the answer. “Good. Supervisor Maddox, your cargo has been delivered and your Worker has made her choice. You may return to your settlement with whatever explanations you think appropriate.”

Maddox’s face cycled through several expressions before settling on resigned anger. “This is highly irregular. There will be consequences.”

“There always are,” Kessa replied. “The question is whether they’re consequences worth bearing.”

* * *

As Maddox prepared to leave with the remains of the convoy, Veera felt a moment of vertigo. Everything familiar was disappearing: her transport bike, her role as a Worker, the predictable rhythms that had defined her life since the gene-readers marked her for hauling. Through the open doors she watched him mount his gleaming, useless bike in the last red light, his torn silk sail hanging like a flag of some country she no longer lived in.

“Second thoughts?” Kessa asked.

“No. Just adjusting to the fall.”

“Good answer.” Kessa laughed. “Fall is how we learn to fly. Come, let me show you where you’ll be working.”

She led Veera deeper into the workshop, past the master crafters and their incomprehensible projects, to a smaller space that felt like a workshop within a workshop. It was a single room of stone through a low doorway in the back wall, its ceiling lower than the great hall’s, its two high windows cut narrow so that the light came down in blades. Here, the tools were simpler but no less precise: hammers for shaping, files for smoothing, measuring devices that could gauge tolerances to fractions of inches, all of them arranged on the walls with a care that bordered on reverence.

“This is where our trials take place,” Kessa explained. “Away from the distractions of the main forge, where you can focus entirely on the conversation between your vision and the metal.” She gestured toward a collection of raw materials. Steel tubing, gear assemblies, chain links, wire for cable systems. “Everything you need to build a complete bike, plus access to the sail-makers for cloth and rigging.”

“Where do I start?”

“With understanding. What kind of rider are you? What terrain will you face? What dangers must you overcome? The bike you build should be an extension of yourself. Not a generic solution, but your specific answer to your specific needs.”

Veera closed her eyes. She tried to envision herself riding. Not the grinding labor of cargo hauling, but the kind of riding her grandmother had described: swift movement across open ground, the harmony between human will and mechanical response that transformed travel into art.

But she also remembered the Stone-Walker attack. The split-second decisions. Whatever she built would need to handle combat as well as speed. Would need to be ready for the dangers Tlacaelel had hinted at, the stirring of old hungers in the northern reaches.

“I need something fast,” she said, opening her eyes. “But also strong. Something that can carry cargo when necessary but fight when required.” She paused, reaching for words that matched the shape forming in her mind. “Something that remembers what the Sacred Horses were. Partners, not just tools.”

Kessa nodded. “A war-horse that can also be a work-horse.” She moved to the steel tubing and selected several pieces. “Start with the frame geometry. This will determine everything else. How the bike handles, how weight distributes, how forces transfer from rider to road.”

Veera lifted one of the tubes and felt its weight settle into her grip. The steel was alive under her hands, waiting to be shaped into purpose. For the first time since her grandmother’s death, she felt connected to something larger than her assigned role.

“The frame,” she said. “Like the skeleton of a horse.”

“Exactly. And just like a horse’s bones, every angle and curve affects how the whole body moves. Too steep and it becomes unstable. Too shallow and it loses responsiveness. Finding the right balance, that’s where art meets engineering.”

Veera began to work, measuring and cutting and slowly assembling the fundamental structure that would define her creation. The hammering from the main workshop provided rhythm, a mechanical heartbeat that seemed to pulse in time with her own enhanced cardiovascular system. Through the narrow windows the last of the light went from bronze to gray, and someone she never saw lit the lamps in the corridor.

As the day wore on and her frame began to take shape, she found herself thinking about the larger patterns Tlacaelel had described. The movements in the north. The choice between sustainable harmony and destructive power. Whatever she built here wouldn’t just be her personal mount. It would be preparation for challenges no one could fully predict.

The trial had begun.

Chapter 3

Eyes in the Forge

Steel tubing looked different when it was yours to ruin.

Veera stood in the trial workshop the next morning as dawn light filtered through narrow windows cut high in the stone walls. She had slept here, or tried to, on a blanket Kessa had left folded on the workbench without comment. The compound had woken around her in stages while the windows were still dark: a bell somewhere that rang four times and was answered by no voice at all, then boots in the corridors, then the great bellows of the main forge taking its first breaths of the day, slow and enormous, like something surfacing. By the time the light came, the hammering had already found its rhythm, and the trial workshop’s silence sat inside that rhythm the way a held note sits inside a song. The space felt different empty. Smaller. The tools and raw materials that had seemed full of possibility the evening before now sat in their careful arrangement like a test she had not yet begun to understand.

The raw materials waited. Tubing of different gauges and compositions. Gear assemblies ranging from simple fixed-cog setups to intricate multi-ratio systems. Chain links. Cable wire. Bolts and brackets in wooden boxes sorted by size. Everything she needed to build a complete bicycle, and absolutely no instructions for how to begin.

She picked up a length of chromoly tubing and rolled it between her palms. The metal was cold and patient. Her grandmother had taught her how to ride, how to feel the conversation between body and machine. But building was something else.

She selected a mitering file and set the tube against the bench vice. The angle needed to be precise if the head tube was going to accept the down tube at the correct geometry. She had watched enough maintenance crews to know the theory. But metal resists when you cut against its grain, and a file chatters when your angle drifts even slightly, and theory had not prepared her for either. The chatter went up through the handle and into the bones of her hand, a stuttering complaint she could not smooth no matter how she set her feet, and the cut face came away silvered with tiny ridges where it should have come away clean. Her first attempt produced a joint that gaped on one side. She tried again. Better, but still imprecise. A third time. The gap narrowed but did not close. Each failed cut consumed material she could not replace.

By mid-morning, she had produced exactly one usable joint and wasted enough tubing to build half a frame. A row of short cut ends stood in the scrap box like grave markers, and the skin over her knuckles had opened in two places where the file had jumped its line. Her lungs could process thin prairie air at volume. Her heart could sustain effort for hours without complaint. None of that mattered here. Large lungs and a powerful heart were advantages for hauling cargo across open plains. They contributed nothing to the delicate negotiation between file and steel.

Patience, she told herself. The wind doesn’t arrive all at once either.

She set down the file and pressed her palms flat against the workbench. The wood was warm from the morning sun, and beneath it she could feel the faint vibration of the main forge operating somewhere deeper in the compound. Hammers falling in rhythm. The pulse of creation that never stopped in this place.

She closed her eyes and let her hands rest on the tubing again. Not to cut or file or force, but simply to hold. And something shifted. The tube’s weight settled into her grip with a rightness that her conscious mind couldn’t explain, and her fingers moved to a spot on its length that felt like the place where it wanted to be joined.

She opened her eyes and measured the point her fingers had found. It was three millimeters off from her original calculation. She filed the new angle, and when she pressed the two tubes together, they met with a closeness that surprised her.

She stared at the joint for a long time. Then she picked up the next tube and closed her eyes again.

* * *

The second occupant of the trial workshop arrived shortly after midday, carrying himself with the particular tension of someone pretending not to care about something that mattered enormously.

She heard him in the corridor before she saw him: a single-speed chain tensioned so perfectly it made almost no sound at all.

He was young, perhaps Veera’s age, with the ritual scars of the Honor Society tracing geometric patterns along his forearms. His tools were beautiful: a matched set of files and hammers with handles wrapped in dyed leather, each one bearing the stamp of a master crafter. He arranged them on the opposite workbench with the care of someone performing a ceremony, then stood back and regarded his workspace as though it had personally wronged him.

Veera watched from behind her growing collection of test joints. He hadn’t acknowledged her.

“You are Veera.” He said it to his tools rather than to her. “The cargo Worker.”

“And you are?”

“Orin. This is my second trial.” He pulled a sheet of calculations from his vest and pinned it to the wall above his bench. The page was dense with frame geometry, stress equations, load distribution models. Every number precise. Every line ruled straight. “I failed the first time because my chain tension was point-three percent outside tolerance. Point-three percent.“

“That seems very close to passing.”

“Close is a canyon in the Honor Society.” He selected a tube and began measuring with instruments that could gauge fractions she had no names for. “Forgemaster Kessa does not grade on proximity to success. You either understand the partnership or you do not.”

They worked in silence for an hour. Orin’s technique was immaculate, each movement following a sequence that had clearly been drilled into him since childhood. His joints were mathematically perfect. His frame angles matched his calculations to tolerances Veera couldn’t achieve on her best attempt. He built with the certainty of someone following a sacred text.

But Veera noticed something. When he test-fitted his tubes, he didn’t hold them. He measured them. He checked numbers against his sheet, adjusted with calibrated tools, verified against formulas. The steel never rested in his hands long enough to warm.

She was struggling with her own seat-tube angle when the observation escaped her mouth before she could catch it. “Your down tube is fighting your head tube.”

Orin’s hands stopped. He turned with an expression balanced between offense and curiosity. “My geometry is calculated to the third decimal.”

“I know. But feel how the weight transfers when you press here.” She reached across and pushed gently on the junction where his down tube met the bottom bracket. The frame flexed in a way that measurements wouldn’t catch. A slight unwillingness, as though the metal was complying under protest.

Orin stared at the flex point. Then at her. “How did you know that?”

Veera pulled her hand back. “My grandmother used to say that wind has opinions about where it wants to go. You can measure its speed and direction, but you also have to feel its mood. Metal might be the same.”

“Metal doesn’t have moods.”

“Maybe not. But you’ve been measuring that joint for twenty minutes without touching it longer than you need to take a reading.”

Something shifted behind his eyes. He placed both hands flat on his frame and held them there, waiting. After a long moment, he adjusted the down tube angle by a degree and a half. No calculation. Just response.

The frame settled into itself with a sound like a held breath releasing.

“Point noted,” Orin said, and returned to his calculations without further comment. But his hands lingered on the steel a little longer after each measurement, and Veera caught him once pressing his palm flat against a tube with his eyes closed, testing something that had no column on his specification sheet.

* * *

She first noticed the woman at the edge of her vision, standing in the workshop doorway like a shadow that had decided to observe rather than follow.

The woman was older than Kessa, perhaps fifty, with close-cropped silver hair and a face that had been weathered by decades of wind and hard decisions. She was built narrow through the shoulders but dense, compact, and she stood with her weight settled low, one hip cocked against the door frame, her feet positioned to move in any direction without shifting first. She wore Honor Society leathers, but they had been modified in ways that would make a traditionalist wince. Pockets stitched from mismatched fabrics. Tool loops made of salvaged cable. A collar reinforced with riveted scrap metal in patterns that spoke of Scavenger origin, worn openly, without apology.

She watched Veera work for ten minutes without speaking, then left.

She returned the next morning. Same position. Same silence. Her eyes tracked Veera’s hands with an attention so focused it had physical weight.

Veera waited until the woman’s gaze settled on a joint she was filing. Then she spoke without looking up. “You’re making me cut crooked.”

“If a pair of eyes throws off your filing, you have larger problems than I can solve.” The voice was dry, amused, and entirely unapologetic.

“I didn’t ask you to solve anything. I’m telling you that standing in doorways staring at people is unusual behavior, even for the Honor Society.”

The woman laughed. It was short and rough, like a gear engaging unexpectedly. “You’ve been here two days and you’re already an expert on our behavior.”

Veera set down her file and turned. Up close, the woman’s modifications were even more striking. Her Honor Society bike, visible in the corridor behind her, carried Scavenger innovations that most compound residents would consider heretical: a multi-gear drivetrain where tradition demanded single-speed purity, handlebars wrapped in repurposed rubber rather than ceremonial leather, and a frame that had been cut and re-welded at least twice to accommodate a rider who apparently changed her mind about geometry the way other people changed clothes.

“You crossed over,” Veera said. It wasn’t a question.

“Everyone crosses something.” The woman leaned against the door frame with the ease of someone who had mastered the art of looking casual while remaining perfectly alert. “I’m Sable. I assess threats for the compound. Patterns, movements, things that don’t fit where they’re placed. It’s my job to watch.”

“And I don’t fit where I’m placed.”

“Not even slightly. Which makes you either a threat worth monitoring or a development worth understanding.” She straightened, and the shift was subtle but total, the casual lean replaced by something coiled and precise. “I haven’t decided which.”

“Could you decide from inside the workshop? The light is better, and I have a question about crossbar stress distribution in asymmetric frames.”

Sable’s eyebrows lifted. A beat of silence. Then: “Asymmetric frames are a Scavenger indulgence. The Honor Society builds in bilateral symmetry.”

“The Honor Society didn’t grow up hauling unbalanced cargo loads across terrain that shifts under your wheels. I need a frame that handles weight distribution changes without losing stability. Symmetry is elegant, but my riding life has never been symmetrical.”

Another beat. Sable’s eyes moved from Veera’s face to her hands, which were still dusty with steel filings and marked with the small cuts of a beginner finding her way. Something in the older woman’s expression changed, though Veera couldn’t name what it was.

“Your crossbar is too long for the load variance you’re describing. Shorten it by eight centimeters, add a secondary gusset at the seat-tube junction, and use a thinner gauge for the top tube so it flexes instead of transmitting shock to the headset.” She delivered this in the tone of someone reciting weather conditions. Facts, not favors.

Then she left.

Veera picked up her file and began reworking the crossbar. Eight centimeters shorter. The adjustment was specific, confident, and exactly right.

She would be back in that doorway tomorrow. Veera was certain of it. And she would have another question ready.

* * *

Night in the Honor Society compound carried a different kind of silence than the plains.

On the open ground, silence was an absence. Wind dying, animals settling, the world holding its breath between one event and the next. Here, silence was constructed. Carefully maintained. The forges banked to embers, the apprentices in their quarters following schedules that governed sleep as precisely as they governed waking labor. Even the insects seemed to respect the compound’s rhythms, their songs muted within the earthwork walls.

Veera sat on the workshop floor with her back against the bench and the carved horse in her hands. Tlacaelel’s gift. She turned it slowly, feeling where the carver’s knife had followed the grain of the wood rather than fighting it. The mane that became spokes. The hooves that became pedals. Two forms of movement captured in one small figure, as though the carver had understood that the old partnerships and the new ones were not separate things but the same impulse expressed across centuries.

Her frame stood propped against the bench beside her, half-finished and honest about it. The joints she had filed by feel were cleaner than the ones she had measured. Orin’s observation about the down tube sat in her mind alongside Sable’s correction of the crossbar, two pieces of knowledge from two very different sources that her hands would integrate tomorrow.

Through the workshop’s high windows, she could see the compound’s nighttime architecture. Guard posts where sentries watched the plains with a discipline that bordered on ritual. Storage buildings locked and sealed with mechanisms she didn’t recognize. In the trade-yard between them stood a single visiting cart, high-wheeled and salt-stained, its running lamps giving off a still green light that did not flicker the way flame did, and Veera looked at it for a while without any idea what she was looking at. And at the center, the ceremonial hall where passage-rites determined who a rider would become, its roof curved like the back of a resting animal, its entrance flanked by torches that were never allowed to die.

Beautiful. Ordered. Fragile.

She had watched the compound for two days now with eyes trained by her grandmother and sharpened by Sable’s example, and she was beginning to see what she suspected Sable saw. The small hesitations in the guard rotations that suggested understaffing. The deference younger crafters showed to tradition even when it produced inferior results. The way Forgemaster Kessa’s authority, while absolute in the workshop, seemed to meet invisible walls when it reached the council chambers.

These were not her problems. She was here to build a bicycle and prove she was more than her genetic caste. That was enough. That should be enough.

But the carved horse in her hands held a question older than the trial, and the unfinished frame beside her felt less like a test and more like preparation for something she couldn’t yet name.

She set the horse on the workbench beside her frame, two incomplete things keeping each other company. Then she lay down on the workshop floor, wrapped in a blanket that smelled of steel filings and plant oil, and let the compound’s constructed silence carry her toward sleep.

Tomorrow she would build. She would ask Sable another question. She would watch Orin discover whatever it was his hands were learning to say. And she would keep her eyes open for the patterns that connected everything, because that was what her grandmother had taught her to do, and some teachings survive every attempt to breed them out.

The frame waited beside her in the dark, its unfinished geometry holding the shape of something that wanted to run.

Chapter 4

The Council’s Eye

The frame was beginning to look like something that could move.

Veera stood back from the workbench in the early light and studied what three days of filed joints and closed eyes and aching hands had produced. The geometry was imperfect by any standard Orin would recognize, but the tubes sat together with a settled quality she had learned to trust. The head tube angled forward at a pitch that felt right when she gripped the space where handlebars would go, and the seat tube rose at an angle that matched the particular way her weight distributed when she leaned into a pedal stroke. It was not a beautiful frame. It was an honest one.

Orin arrived early, for the first time beating the dawn. He moved to his bench without greeting, but as he unpacked his tools he glanced at Veera’s frame, and the glance lasted longer than it had the day before. His own frame was nearly complete, and it was a precise and elegant thing, every joint mitered to tolerances that made Veera’s work look rough by comparison. But she noticed he had changed his down-tube angle again overnight. The adjustment was small. She doubted he had calculated it.

They worked in silence. Veera was fitting her bottom bracket assembly when Orin spoke.

“Your seat-tube junction.” He spoke to his own work, the way he had spoken her name the first time. “The gusset Sable suggested. You filed it at an angle that doesn’t match the tube gauge.”

Veera looked. He was right. The gusset was slightly off-axis, a product of filing by feel on material that didn’t forgive drift. “Thank you.”

Orin nodded once and returned to his chain assembly. It was the first piece of technical help he had offered her. She filed the correction and felt the junction tighten.

By mid-morning, her frame needed only the drivetrain and sail rigging to be considered complete. Orin was further along, already tensioning his chain and adjusting the micro-tolerances that had failed him on his first trial. The workshop smelled of steel filings and sweat and the plant oil Veera used to keep her frame’s joints from seizing. It smelled, she realized, like the beginning of something.

Then she heard the footsteps in the corridor, and the beginning ended.

* * *

There were three of them, and they did not knock.

The door swung to the wall. The stranger came down the steps first, a broad Forgemaster at his shoulder, and neither of them looked to see where the other would stand.

The first was a man Veera had never seen, tall and narrow-shouldered, with the clean hands and unmarked skin of someone who had never shaped metal or hauled cargo or done anything that left evidence on the body. He wore a gray coat cut close at the collar, and from a chain around his neck hung a device larger and more complex than Maddox’s wrist monitor, a flat disc of dark metal covered in etchings too fine to read at a distance. A gene-reader. A real one, not a field supervisor with a borrowed tool, but someone whose entire function was the cataloguing and categorizing of human capability.

The second was a woman who carried herself the way Kessa did, with the authority of craft mastery, but whose energy was colder, more contained. She was broad-faced and heavy-armed, her ritual scars older and more numerous than any Veera had seen in the compound. A Forgemaster. Not Kessa.

She was a head taller than Kessa, with iron-gray hair cropped close to her skull and skin weathered to the color of old leather. Her eyes were a flat slate that gave nothing back.

The third was Kessa herself, walking half a step behind the other two with an expression Veera had not seen on her before. Controlled fury.

The gene-reader’s eyes found Veera immediately. Not her face. Her shoulders. Her collarbones. The places where her streaks would be visible if her collar shifted. He looked at her the way a farmer looks at a plow-animal that has wandered out of its pen.

“So this is the Worker.” His voice was quiet, precise, and carried no particular inflection. He lifted the disc from his chain and held it toward her the way someone might hold up a lantern in a dark room. The device’s etchings caught the workshop light, and Veera felt something she couldn’t explain: a faint pressure behind her sternum, as though the disc was reading her through the air between them. “Veera. Batch designation NR-7. Cardiovascular index ninety-three, which is excellent for a hauling line. Pulmonary capacity in the ninety-eighth percentile.” He lowered the disc. “Supervisor Maddox filed a formal deviation report. Your settlement has requested your return.”

“The settlement can request,” Kessa said from behind him. “The Honor Society has granted a trial. The trial is in progress.”

The second Forgemaster moved past the gene-reader and walked to Veera’s workbench. She studied the frame with hands clasped behind her back, leaning close but not touching, the way someone examines evidence they intend to use. Then she crossed to Orin’s bench and performed the same inspection. Orin stood very still.

“Forgemaster Ulla,” Kessa said, and the name came out with the particular care people use for colleagues they don’t trust. “The trial workshop is my jurisdiction.”

“Your jurisdiction, Kessa. The Council’s oversight.” Ulla’s voice was deeper than Kessa’s and carried the deliberate patience of someone making an argument they considered already won. She straightened from Orin’s bench and turned to face the room. “The Trial of Understanding exists to identify riders who demonstrate mastery of the bond between human and machine. It has criteria. Standards developed over generations. It is not an open invitation extended to anyone who stumbles through our gates with a dented transport and a compelling story.”

“She didn’t stumble. She fought.”

“She improvised. Improvisation is not mastery.” Ulla gestured toward Veera’s frame without looking at it. “This trial has always served riders who have trained from childhood in the fundamentals of frame construction, metallurgy, and rider-mount dynamics. Orin here is on his second attempt after years of preparation. What foundation does this Worker bring? Cargo strapping? Wind-sail adjustment?”

The words landed in the workshop like dropped tools. Orin’s jaw tightened, though Veera couldn’t tell whether it was because Ulla had used him as a prop or because the question was one he had asked himself.

The gene-reader spoke again, quieter. “The concern is not personal, Forgemaster Kessa. The concern is systemic. The caste designations exist because specialization preserves life. Workers are optimized for sustained physical output. Their cardiovascular enhancements, their skeletal modifications, their respiratory capacity, all of it calibrated to a function. When we redirect a specialized organism away from its function, we don’t liberate it. We destabilize the system that depends on it.”

He said organism. Veera heard it and felt the carved horse in her pocket like a coal.

The room had grown warm with argument, and every face in it had changed in some way, Kessa’s darkening, Ulla’s set, Orin’s gone carefully blank. Against the gene-reader’s gray coat, the disc pulsed on, slow and even, at the same unhurried time it had kept since he entered.

Kessa stepped forward until she was standing between the gene-reader and Veera’s workbench. “Let me show you something.” She picked up one of Veera’s test joints from the first day, the ones filed by feel after she closed her eyes. Then she picked up one of the measured joints from before that moment. She held them out, one in each hand. “Tell me which joint was made by the trained craftsperson and which was made by the cargo Worker.”

Ulla took both joints and examined them. She turned each one against the light, and then, briefly, closed her hand around each in turn, the way a smith weighs a thing her eyes have finished with. Her expression did not change, but she held the feel-filed joint longer.

“The trial exists to find understanding,” Kessa said. “Not to confirm what we already believe. If it can only be passed by people we’ve already trained, it isn’t a test. It’s a ceremony.”

Silence held the room. Then the gene-reader looked at Ulla, and some communication passed between them that Veera could not read.

“The trial may proceed,” Ulla said. “Under Council observation. Both candidates will present their completed bikes tomorrow morning. The evaluation will be conducted according to standard criteria, with the Council present. You remain the evaluator, Kessa. But the Council will witness, and the Council will remember.”

The criteria were designed for Honor Society riders. The Council’s presence would make every judgment Kessa rendered a political act. And the word candidates, applied equally to Orin and Veera, turned the trial into a competition that had never been intended.

Ulla and the gene-reader left without acknowledging Veera directly. Kessa remained for a moment, looking at the two frames on their respective benches.

“Build the best bike you can build. That’s all I’ve ever asked of anyone in this room.”

Then she left too, and the workshop was quiet again, except that the quiet was a different shape than before.

* * *

For a long time neither of them moved. Then Orin picked up his chain-tension gauge and set it down again without using it.

“I didn’t ask for that,” he said.

“I know.”

“If I pass because the Council weighted the evaluation against you, it means nothing. It would be worse than failing on chain tension. At least that failure was mine.”

Veera looked at him. He was staring at his frame.

“Then build something that passes on its own terms,” she said. “And I’ll do the same.”

He met her eyes for the first time since the confrontation. Whatever he saw there, it seemed to settle something in him. He nodded once, the same single nod he had given when she corrected his gusset, and returned to his chain assembly.

They worked through the afternoon with a focus that felt different from the days before. The shared silence was still there, but it had an edge now, the awareness that tomorrow they would be measured against each other by people who had already decided what the measurement should prove.

Veera fitted her chain ring and threaded the chain through the rear derailleur with hands that were steadier than she expected. The gene-reader’s words kept surfacing. Batch designation NR-7. A number. A category. She had been catalogued before she’d ever spoken a word or held a tool, and the man in the gray coat had carried her entire biological identity on a disc around his neck the way other people carried keepsakes. He had known her lungs better than she knew them herself. He had not known, and could not know, what her hands had learned in this room.

She tightened the chain tension by feel, then checked it with Orin’s gauge, which he had left on the dividing line between their workspaces without comment. The tension was within tolerance. Not perfect, but within. She adjusted it a quarter-turn further and felt the chain settle into the gears with a faint click that traveled up through the frame and into her palms.

Orin’s hands moved with more deliberation than before, testing each adjustment twice where he used to trust it once. And occasionally, when one of them solved a problem worth noticing, the other paused just long enough to register it before returning to their own work.

* * *

Sable appeared in the doorway at dusk, which was late for her.

She leaned against the frame with her usual economy of posture, but something was different. Her eyes moved to Veera’s bench, scanned the progress, then moved to the corridor behind her and back. She was checking sight lines, Veera realized. Making sure no one was close enough to hear.

“Your headset bearing is seated too high. Drop it two millimeters or your steering will bind under load.”

Veera adjusted the bearing. “Thank you.”

Sable watched her work the adjustment, then spoke again in the same flat tone. “The northern watch has doubled its rotation. Two extra riders posted to the wall since yesterday. Kessa hasn’t been told why.”

The words sat in the air between them. Veera’s hands stopped moving.

“Scouts?”

“One came in this morning before dawn. Went straight to the Council chambers. Hasn’t come out.” Sable’s voice hadn’t changed, but her weight had shifted forward onto the balls of her feet, the casual lean gone. “The Council spent their morning arguing about whether a Worker belongs in a forge. Meanwhile, they have a scout behind closed doors with news they haven’t shared with the Forgemasters.”

She let that settle. Then she straightened, and the coiled readiness slid back behind the mask of ease.

“Finish your bike, Veera. Finish it well.”

She left. In the corridor, her footsteps were silent.

* * *

Orin left at full dark, his frame complete. He paused at the door and looked back at Veera, who was still working by the light of a single oil lamp.

“For what it’s worth,” he said, “your frame has something mine doesn’t. I can’t calculate what it is, and that bothers me.”

“Good.”

The ghost of a smile crossed his face, there and gone. “Goodnight, Veera.”

“Goodnight, Orin.”

Alone, she worked. The oil lamp threw long shadows across the bench and the silence of the empty workshop settled around her like water filling a vessel. The stone floor gave up the day’s heat slowly, and she worked in the lamp’s small country of light with the dark standing all around it. Without Orin’s precise movements to measure herself against, she fell entirely into the rhythm her hands had been teaching her all week. Cut by feel. File by feel. Let the metal tell you where it wants to go.

The drivetrain went in with a smoothness that surprised her, the chain finding its tension on the second attempt, the gears meshing with a sound that was closer to music than mechanics. She sat on the floor and listened to that sound, turning the pedal crank slowly with one hand, letting the chain run through its path. Something in the way it moved reminded her of the transport wheels on the open plain, that harmonic hum that rose from the spokes. The same principle at a different scale. Motion wanting to be free.

She rigged the sail mounts last. This was the work she knew best, though she had never done it on a frame this light. Years of adjusting cargo sails in variable wind had taught her things no Honor Society manual contained: how cloth behaves when load shifts unpredictably, how a boom needs room to swing when the rider’s weight moves, how to rig for a wind that changes its mind. She adapted those principles to a frame that was smaller, quicker, built for response rather than endurance, and when she tightened the last cable and felt the sail system respond to a tug on the rudder lever, the vibration that traveled through the frame felt like a conversation beginning.

When she finished, she stood back. The bike leaned against the bench in the lamplight, and it was not the bike she had imagined. It was stranger and less refined than anything the Honor Society would consider standard. The asymmetric crossbar gave it an off-balance look, like an animal favoring one leg. The sail mounts were positioned for a rider who expected wind from unpredictable directions. The frame geometry was a conversation between Orin’s precision, Sable’s practicality, and something older that Veera’s hands had contributed without her permission.

It was not beautiful. But when she gripped the handlebars and pressed her weight into the frame, it answered her. Not perfectly. Not yet. But it answered.

She set the carved horse on the seat, a small rider on a strange mount, and sat down on the workshop floor. Tomorrow the Council would watch and judge. Tomorrow the gene-reader’s categories would press against Kessa’s conviction that understanding transcends engineering. Tomorrow Orin would present a frame built on years of training, and Veera would present one built on three days and the memory of grandmothers.

And somewhere beyond the compound walls, the northern watch kept its doubled vigil, and a scout sat in a closed room telling the Council something they hadn’t shared with anyone else.

Veera lay down on her blanket, the bike above her casting its strange shadow on the wall. The gene-reader’s word sat behind her sternum like a stone. Organism. She pressed her palm flat against the workshop floor, the way she had pressed it against the tubing three days ago, and felt the compound breathing around her.

The wind outside had shifted. It came from the north now, and it carried a smell she didn’t recognize.

Chapter 5

What the Wind Carried

The smell had come on the wind two nights running. Faint, then gone, then faint again, a tang that none of the older crafters could name and none of them liked. The northern watch had doubled the morning before, and no one had told Veera why. She had not asked. A Worker who asked questions in someone else’s compound did not stay long enough to hear the answers.

The trial would go ahead. That was all anyone would say. The compound had stood through three generations of raids, and it did not break a rite for a smell on the wind.

But the morning wore the smell the way skin wears a bruise. Veera walked her bike out from the trial workshop through corridors where apprentices ate standing up, bowls in one hand, eyes going to the high windows between bites. The wall shifts had changed over early; she passed two riders coming down from the north tower who did not speak to each other or to anyone. In the forge-yard the hammers rang on schedule, but under them the compound’s other sounds had thinned, the loose talk, the laughter from the apprentice quarters, the whistling, all of it gone quiet the way birds go quiet, and nobody remarking on it, and everybody hearing it.

The testing ground was an open oval of packed earth at the compound’s eastern edge, sheltered from the worst of the wind by berms of piled earth and stone but exposed enough that a rider could catch a gust and use it. Veera had watched trials here from the corridor on her second day. Now she stood at its center with her bike beside her and the Council seated above her on a raised platform of timber and stone at the oval’s northern edge, set high enough that they looked down on the course as if reading a map.

She had learned, in her days inside the walls, that the Honor Society was governed by seven Forgemasters. Five of them sat in judgment that morning, ranged along the platform’s long bench. Ulla, arms crossed, her chin already lifted against a verdict no one had yet given. Beside her sat a woman with hair the color of old steel and a stillness that had not bent in fifty years, whose gaze found Veera and weighed her without heat. Three others Veera did not know, older men and women with the composed patience of those accustomed to judging. The gene-reader stood among them but slightly apart, his disc resting against his coat, there to certify what the Council ruled and not to rule it.

Kessa stood apart from them all at ground level, near the testing oval’s edge, a position that was technically correct for the evaluator but placed her below the Council in every way that mattered. She made the sixth. The seventh chair sat somewhere Veera had not been shown, and no one on the platform spoke of why it stood empty.

Orin stood across the oval with his bike. He had polished the frame until it caught the morning light, and the geometry was so clean it looked drawn rather than built. It carried the lines of a racing bike with the weight of a ceremonial frame, the kind of build the Honor Society’s apprentice songs called a true-bike, made to last a rider’s life. He met Veera’s eyes briefly. Neither of them spoke.

Apprentices and crafters had gathered along the earthwork berms that ringed the testing ground, more than Veera had expected. The oldest of them had brought their hammers, unasked, and held them head-down at their sides, the way tools were carried in a procession. Word had traveled. The Worker in the forge. The trial the Council had come to watch. She could feel their attention like heat.

“Orin of the Honor Society,” Kessa called. “Present your work.”

He rode the way he built: with precision that bordered on reverence. His wheels ran almost silent, trued the way the Honor Society trued its racing wheels, so that what crossed the testing ground was the hush of tires on packed earth and nothing else. The testing oval had a standard course marked with posts and packed-earth ramps that required acceleration, braking, cornering, and a sustained climb up a graded ramp. Orin’s bike moved through each element with a mechanical grace that drew murmurs from the apprentices on the berms. His chain tension was perfect. His sail caught wind at exactly the angles his calculations had predicted. His cornering was tight and controlled, the frame responding to inputs with the fidelity of a well-tuned instrument.

But Veera, watching from across the oval, saw something else too. On the sustained climb, when the ramp steepened and the effort shifted from technique to something less measurable, Orin’s hands changed. His grip softened. His weight settled lower into the frame. For three seconds, maybe four, he rode the way Veera rode, not from calculation but from the body’s own knowing. Then his hands tightened again and the precision returned, but those three seconds had been there, and Veera had seen them.

The Council nodded among themselves. Ulla’s expression carried the satisfaction of a point proven. This was what the trial was supposed to produce.

“Veera,” Kessa called. No title. No caste. Just the name. “Present your work.”

She swung onto the saddle and the gene-reader’s disc flashed once on his chest. Reading her. Let him read, she thought, and tightened her grip on the handlebars.

The bike was strange to look at, she knew that. The frame was unpainted steel that had taken on the dull blue-gray of worked metal, and its lines did not match the symmetry that the Honor Society prized. The crossbar ran at an angle rather than straight, slightly higher on the left than the right. The wheels were double-spoked in opposing patterns, the way war-bikes were laced, because Sable had insisted that a frame meant for unpredictable wind would meet unpredictable ground, and a single bent spoke should not be allowed to take down a wheel. Above the rear wheel rose the mast and sail rig that no Honor Society bike carried as standard, a triangle of grass-cloth weathered to the color of dried straw, smaller than a transport sail but rigged to swing on a boom the way her transport’s had. It looked, she thought, like something a Worker had built. Which it was.

It felt different from the transport. Lighter, obviously, but it was more than weight. The frame responded to shifts in her body that the cargo hauler would have ignored. When she leaned, it leaned. When her hips moved, the steering answered. The asymmetric crossbar, which had looked ungainly on the workbench, made sudden sense in motion: it accommodated the way her weight naturally distributed, the slight imbalance that years of hauling uneven cargo loads had built into her posture.

She entered the course. The first elements were simple and she handled them adequately, nothing more. Her cornering was wider than Orin’s, her braking less precise. Her left hand rode the rudder lever the whole way, holding its small running argument with a morning wind that had nothing much to say, and through the grips she could feel the frame asking her for something the course was not giving it. She could feel the Council’s assessment forming: competent, perhaps, but rough. Unfinished. A Worker’s effort.

Then the wind shifted.

It announced itself in the grass first, a hiss running down the slope beyond the northern berm, and the banners over the platform swung their tails around a half-breath before the air arrived. Veera’s left hand moved before the rest of her knew why. It came from the north, a gust that cut across the testing oval at an angle the course hadn’t been designed for. Several apprentices on the berms grabbed their hats. On the Council platform, coats flapped. And Veera’s sail, rigged for variable wind by hands that had spent years reading the prairie’s moods, caught the gust and channeled it.

The bike surged. Not the controlled acceleration of Orin’s test run, but something wilder, the frame absorbing the sudden force and translating it into forward motion that carried her up the graded ramp at a speed the course wasn’t built to accommodate. The boom swung out full against its stays, the grass-cloth went taut as a drumhead, and the double-laced wheels climbed through their two braided notes into a chord she had never ridden fast enough to hear. Her body adjusted without thought, her weight shifting to keep the frame stable, her hands making corrections she couldn’t have calculated, the same deep knowing that had guided her file to the right spot on the tubing, that had driven her transport into a Stone-Walker’s stilts.

She crested the ramp and banked hard, using the remaining wind to carve a line back across the oval that was not in the standard course, that broke the pattern of the test entirely. The bike held. The sail spilled wind at exactly the right moment. The asymmetric frame absorbed the lateral force and kept her upright through a turn that would have thrown a symmetrical build sideways.

She came to a stop in the center of the oval, breathing hard, the gene-reader’s disc pulsing steadily against his coat.

Silence on the berms. Silence on the platform.

Then Orin, from across the oval, said quietly: “What was that?”

* * *

The Council could not settle on what they had just watched. Veera could see it on their faces from the center of the oval: the older men leaning toward each other in tight conversation, the gene-reader’s mouth set in a thin line, Ulla on her feet before anyone had spoken.

Kessa walked the course with both bikes, testing joints, checking chain tension, examining sail rigging. She took her time. The Council watched from the platform with the restlessness of people who wanted to speak and were being made to wait.

When she finished, she stood between the two bikes and addressed the platform.

“Orin’s frame meets every standard criterion. Chain tension within tolerance. Geometry sound. Sail integration follows established principles. His ride demonstrated control, consistency, and mechanical understanding. He passes the Trial of Understanding.”

Orin closed his eyes. When he opened them, they were bright with tears he did not let fall.

“Veera’s frame,” Kessa continued, and the testing ground went still again, “does not meet standard criteria in several areas. The crossbar is asymmetric. The geometry deviates from established ratios. The sail rigging follows no documented pattern.”

Ulla’s chin lifted.

“However,” Kessa said, “the Trial of Understanding does not ask whether a bike meets standard criteria. It asks whether the builder demonstrates understanding of the partnership between rider and mount. What this Council just witnessed was a rider and a machine responding to unpredictable conditions as a single unit. That is understanding. Veera passes the Trial of Understanding.”

The platform erupted. Not shouting, not in the Honor Society, but the controlled intensity of people who disagreed profoundly and were accustomed to having their disagreements matter.

Ulla was on her feet. “The criteria exist for reasons, Kessa. You cannot simply override them because you saw something that impressed you.”

“I am the evaluator. The criteria are my instrument, not my cage.”

The gene-reader spoke from where he stood, his quiet voice somehow cutting through the platform’s agitation. “What we witnessed was an anomaly. An organism operating outside its designed parameters. This does not validate the deviation. It raises questions about what we are permitting to develop unchecked within”

He stopped. His eyes had moved past the testing ground, past the berms, past the earthwork walls. He was looking north.

Others followed his gaze. One of the Council members stood. Then another.

Veera turned.

* * *

The northern horizon had changed.

Where the plain should have been flat and featureless, stretching into haze the way it had every morning since Veera first rode across it, there was now a line. Dark and low against the earth, it moved. Not the way wind moves grass or heat moves light. It moved with purpose. A smudge of shapes too distant to identify but too numerous to ignore, advancing across the plain in a formation that had nothing organic about it.

Above the line, smoke. Not the white smoke of cooking fires or the black smoke of forge-work, but something between, a yellowish haze that clung to the ground and spread outward from the advancing shapes like breath from a mouth in winter.

The smell hit next. The same unfamiliar tang that had been in the wind the night before, but stronger now, thick enough to taste. Veera’s enhanced lungs processed it automatically and found nothing they recognized. Not plant oil. Not cooking fuel. Not any of the organic compounds her body had been designed to metabolize. Something older. Something the world had agreed to forget.

No one on the berms spoke. No one on the platform spoke. Somewhere back in the compound a hammer went on ringing, three strokes, four, and stopped, and for the first time since Veera had passed the gates, the giant’s heartbeat was silent. What was left was the wind, and the smell riding it, and a hundred people standing very still on the earthworks, watching the horizon move.

On the platform, the gene-reader’s face had lost its composure. His disc was pulsing rapidly, reading something in the air that Veera couldn’t see.

“Petroleum,” he said. The word fell into the testing ground like a stone into still water. “The Stone-Walkers were right. The dragons have woken.”

* * *

The compound moved. An apprentice ran past the berms with an armload of unstrung bows and no one to hand them to. Two crafters wheeled a quench-tub toward the eastern gate, stopped, argued, and wheeled it back the way they had come. The raid-drill was in everyone’s legs, and everyone’s legs had begun it, and halfway to their stations the riders kept stopping to look north, at the thing no drill had ever imagined, before their legs carried them on.

Kessa was already crossing the testing ground toward the main workshops, shouting orders to apprentices who were running before she finished speaking. Ulla followed half a step behind, the argument between them dissolved by something that made it irrelevant. The Council members descended from the platform in various states of urgency, some moving toward the walls, others toward the council chambers where Veera knew a scout had been sitting behind closed doors for a day and a half. On the berms, the crowd came apart into its separate duties, and a boy who had been cheering minutes before stood alone by the course posts until an older crafter took him by the shoulder and turned him toward the walls.

The northern watch sounded a horn. A deep, sustained note that Veera felt in her chest. Along the compound walls, riders who had been doubled since yesterday now scrambled to triple their positions, blades and tire-irons hanging from their saddles.

Orin stood beside his bike, gripping the handlebars. His knuckles were white. “What is that?”

“What Tlacaelel warned me about,” Veera said.

Sable appeared. Not from a doorway this time but from behind the berms, already moving, her modified leathers cinched tight and her bike rolling beside her. She stopped in front of Veera. Her eyes were steady, and her feet were positioned to move in any direction.

“The scout reported a convoy. Machines powered by combustion, moving south along the old pipeline routes. The Council sat on it for a day hoping it would change direction.” Her voice was the same flat delivery, but faster. “It didn’t.”

“Combustion,” Veera said. The word felt wrong in her mouth, like something her grandmother had warned her never to speak aloud.

“Petroleum engines. The old technology.” Sable’s eyes did not leave the horizon. “They burn the dead earth-blood for power. A single one of those machines can outrun a hundred bicycles for a day before it stops to drink. They don’t tire. They don’t need to eat. They don’t have to read the wind. Whatever they’re carrying north of here, they intend to bring south. And there is nothing between us and them but plain.”

Dead earth-blood. Her grandmother had used the same words, in the same low voice, for the thing the oil-ravens had drunk until the world cracked open. Veera had taken it for a story, the way she had taken the Sacred Horses for a story, until a Stone-Walker dropped a carved horse at her feet. The smell crossing the plain now was not a story.

“How long before they reach us?”

“Hours. Maybe less. They’re faster than anything on legs or wheels.”

Veera looked at the northern horizon. The line of shapes was closer now, or maybe she was just seeing it more clearly. Individual forms resolving out of the haze. Vehicles. The word surfaced from stories her grandmother had told in whispers, the machines that had eaten the old world.

She looked at her bike. The strange, asymmetric, unorthodox thing that had just passed a trial the Council was still arguing about. It was built for unpredictable wind. For shifting loads. For a rider who expected conditions to change without warning.

She looked at Sable. “What do you need?”

Sable’s mouth did not move, but something in her eyes shifted, the way still water shifts when the wind first touches it. “Information. Someone has to ride north and see what’s actually coming before it arrives. How many. How fast. What they’re carrying. The Council won’t send a rider until they’ve finished debating, and by then it won’t matter.”

“You’re asking me.”

“I’m telling you what’s needed. What you do with that is yours.”

Veera’s hand found the carved horse in her pocket. The wood was warm. Her grandmother had a word for that warmth. The gene-readers had none. The wind was coming from the north, carrying that strange petroleum smell across the plain, and her sail was rigged for exactly this kind of wind.

She looked at Orin, who was watching her with an expression she couldn’t read. She looked at the Council, scattered and arguing. She thought of the workshop behind the inner walls, the bench where she had learned to close her eyes and let the metal speak. She could not see it from here, but she could feel it the way she felt the carved horse in her pocket, a presence that did not need to be looked at to be known.

Then she mounted her bike, the one that didn’t meet standard criteria, the one built by an organism operating outside its designed parameters, and pointed it north.

“Veera.” Orin’s voice, behind her. She turned. He was holding out his chain-tension gauge, the one he had left on the line between their workspaces. “Your chain will need adjustment after the first kilometer. It’s new. It stretches.”

She took it. Their fingers touched briefly over the small metal instrument.

“Ride well,” he said.

Veera turned north. The eastern gate stood open, its iron-shod timbers scarred from old raids, and the gatekeeper stood at her counterweighted lever, holding the leaf wide. Four days ago she had opened this gate to let a cargo Worker in. She said nothing now, but as Veera passed she took one hand from the lever and touched two fingers to the scars on her forearm, and Veera carried that the way she carried the tile and the horse, not knowing its name, knowing its weight. Beyond the gate the swept and ordered ground of the compound gave way to open prairie at a line as clear as a shoreline, wild grass beginning where the rake had stopped, in the space of a single wheel-turn. The wind filled her sail, and the bike surged beneath her with the same wild responsiveness it had shown on the testing ramp. Behind her, the Honor Society’s compound grew smaller. Ahead, the shapes on the horizon grew larger.

She rode toward them.

Chapter 6

The Long Sight

The wind held.

Veera rode north out of the compound with the sail bellied tight above the rear wheel, the asymmetric crossbar humming under her hands, the bike answering every shift of her weight as if it had been waiting for this kind of work. The plain opened in front of her, wide and pale under the late morning light, and the petroleum smell came in waves. Stronger when the wind gusted. Thinner when it dropped. But always there.

She had ridden this plain for years under cargo, learning its small hills and the way its grass tilted to mark where water lay close beneath the surface. She knew where the prairie dipped into shallow draws that could hide a rider from a distant watcher. She knew where the old oil-raven wrecks rose out of the earth like broken teeth, salvageable for cover. Her grandmother had taught her to read these things without naming them, and now her hands and her body read them for her, choosing the line her bike took without her permission.

Behind her, the compound shrank. Ahead, the dark line on the horizon did not.

She did not look back. Looking back was for riders who had not yet decided.

* * *

She had been riding for perhaps an hour when she heard the second set of wheels.

They came from her left, fast and quiet, threading along a draw she had marked as good cover. By the time Veera turned her head, Sable was already alongside her, weight low over the frame, her bike running on the multi-gear drivetrain that marked her Scavenger origins more clearly than any patch on her leathers. Honor Society tradition held that a true rider rode one gear well; Scavengers held that a rider rode whatever the terrain asked for. Sable’s bike ate the prairie at a pace Veera’s single-speed could not match without the sail.

“You took longer than I expected,” Veera said.

“I had to argue with the gatekeeper.” Sable’s voice carried the same flat delivery as always, but her breathing was quick. “She wanted a Council authorization. I told her the Council would still be voting on what color to wear when the first machine reached the wall.”

“Did that work?”

“She let me through. I did not ask why.”

They rode side by side for a long moment without speaking, the only sounds were the hum of their wheels in the dry grass and the snap of Veera’s sail when a gust hit it crosswise.

“You did not have to come,” Veera said.

“I know.”

“Then why?”

Sable did not answer for a while. Her eyes were on the horizon, on the dark line that was no longer just a smudge but had begun to resolve into individual shapes: square forms with hard edges, larger than any bike, smaller than the Stone-Walker stilts-houses she had seen in stories.

“I crossed over once,” Sable said finally. “I know what it costs when no one rides with you.”

She did not look at Veera when she said it. Veera did not look at her. The wind carried the petroleum smell between them and neither of them spoke of it.

* * *

They climbed a low rise that Veera had used a hundred times to scout for raiders on the cargo runs. From the top of it, the world opened.

The convoy was no longer a line. It was a procession. Veera counted as she rode the ridge: twelve large machines, each one the size of a small house, riding on wheels that were not wheels in any sense she recognized. They were thick black rings of some material she had no word for, soft-looking but clearly hard, leaving deep parallel scars in the earth behind them. Where the scars ran, the grass had not been bent the way wheels bent it. It had been crushed into the ground and shone wet against the dust, and the two dark lines ran back toward the horizon as far as Veera could see, straight as a rule, deviating for nothing. Between and around the large machines moved smaller ones, two-wheeled like bikes but with no chain that Veera could see, no pedals, no rider’s effort going into the motion at all. The wheels simply turned, powered by the same yellow smoke that fed everything in the convoy. The riders crouched over their handlebars in dark coats and hard helmets, their feet resting on small platforms instead of working. Watching them, Veera felt something her grandmother had described once and never explained: the wrongness of a body that was being carried instead of carrying itself.

And at the center of the procession, drawn by no animal and pushed by no rider, moved something Veera did not immediately understand. A great cylindrical tank, low and wide, riding on its own set of black wheels, its surface dulled by the dust of long travel but unmistakable in its purpose. Fuel. They were carrying their own dead earth-blood with them, enough to feed the machines for a journey that did not end at the next water hole. A walking oil-raven, she thought, and felt the back of her neck go cold. Her grandmother had taught her that the fallen oil-ravens of the Plain of Memories had been the world’s punishment for drinking too deep. This one had not fallen. This one had been brought back to its feet and made to walk.

“They are not raiding,” Veera said. The words came out before she had finished thinking of them.

“No.” Sable was crouched beside her bike, her eyes narrow against the light. “A raid takes what it finds. This is supply. They are bringing what they need to stay.”

They watched in silence. The procession moved with a steadiness that no human-powered convoy could maintain, hour after hour without rest, the machines drinking from the tank when they slowed and rising again with the same indifferent power. Veera tried to imagine what it would feel like to ride a thing that did not tire. She could not. Her body would not give her the picture.

Behind her, a sound she had not noticed before resolved into language. A low rumble that had been beneath everything since they crested the ridge, vibrating up through the earth and into her knees. The machines. They made the ground hum. She listened for a cadence in it, the way she would have listened to a column of riders, the rise and fall of effort, breath, stroke. There was none. The sound ran level and unbroken, without breath in it anywhere.

“We have enough,” Sable said. “Numbers. Direction. Speed. The tank tells us they intend to settle, not pass through. We need to ride back.”

Veera did not move. She was looking at the small two-wheeled machines, the ridden ones, the ones that flanked the convoy and ranged outward in pairs.

“Scouts,” she said.

“Yes.”

“They will have seen us by now.”

“Almost certainly.”

Sable said it without inflection, the way she said everything, but her hand had moved to the tire-iron strapped along her frame. Veera looked east along the ridge. A pair of the small machines had peeled away from the main convoy and turned toward the rise. Their yellow smoke trailed behind them. The rumble of their engines was already louder than it should have been.

* * *

The first arrow came from the wrong direction.

Not from the machines. From above and behind, sailing in a long arc that ended in the dirt three paces from the lead scout’s wheel. The scout swerved hard, and his partner pulled up beside him, and both machines came to a stop with their engines coughing.

Veera looked up.

On the long rise behind them, walking as if the slope did not exist, came a figure she knew. Tlacaelel of the Stone-Walkers, Speaker-of-High-Places, Keeper-of-Rising-Paths, his stilts triple-jointed and silent on the prairie grass, carrying him high above the tallest of them, his geometric face-paint catching the sun. The last time Veera had seen him he had been falling. She had driven her cargo transport into his stilts on the Plain of Memories and brought him down in a tangle of oil-raven steel, and he had risen from the wreck of it laughing, named her Veera-Who-Rides-Steel-Horses, pressed the carved horse into her hand, and told her to watch the north. She had watched it. Now the north had come for them both. Behind him came six others, all on stilts, all bearing the long bows the Stone-Walkers used when they did not want to use harpoons, the arrows tipped in oil-raven steel that caught the light like splinters of old sky.

Sable’s hand had not left the tire-iron, but it had not moved either. She was watching Veera.

“A friend?” she asked.

“Something close to one.”

Tlacaelel descended the rise with the long, swinging gait the Stone-Walkers used for distance, and stopped on the high ground above Veera and Sable. The other six fanned out along the ridge, bows ready, their attention on the two scouts who had stopped their machines at the foot of the slope.

“Veera-Who-Rides-Steel-Horses,” Tlacaelel said, and the warrior-weight he had given her name in their first meeting was still in his voice. “You did not wait for us to find you. This speaks well of your blood-memory.”

“You said you watched the north,” Veera said.

“We watch the north. We have watched it for many seasons.” His painted face hardened, and he looked past her at the convoy moving steadily south. “We have watched them gather. We have watched them dig. We have watched them wake what should have stayed asleep. Today they cross into lands where bicycles still ride, and so today we stop watching alone.”

“The Honor Society does not know what is coming,” Veera said. “Not really. They are still arguing.”

Tlacaelel laughed, the wind-chime laugh she remembered.

“Of course they are. People who have built a wall always argue about whether the danger is real until the danger arrives at the gate.” He looked down at her bike, taking in the asymmetric frame, the rigged sail, the lines that did not match any standard. “You have built your true mount. I see this. Now you must ride it back to your wall and make them understand. We will buy you the time.”

At the foot of the slope, the two petroleum scouts had not moved. Their machines idled, blue smoke rising from the back of each. They were watching the Stone-Walkers on the ridge. The Stone-Walkers were watching them back. The bows did not need to draw to make their meaning known.

“They will report this,” Sable said.

“Yes. And they will know that the high places are not empty. This is a thing they did not know before. It will change their reckoning. But not enough.” Tlacaelel turned back to Veera. “Ride. Ride now. Tell your wall that the petroleum dragons have not only woken, they march in formation. Tell them that the Stone-Walkers ride for the same reason the Sacred Horses once ran: because what comes south does not stop when bicycles do.”

He pressed something into her hand. A small clay tile, no bigger than her thumb, marked with the geometric patterns of his face-paint.

“Show this to your Forgemasters. Those who can read it will read it. Those who cannot, will at least know that you did not invent us.”

* * *

They rode south fast.

Veera let the sail take the wind, which had shifted again and now ran in their favor, pushing them across the plain at a speed she had never reached under cargo. Sable kept pace on her multi-gear, her body low, her breathing measured. They did not speak. There was nothing to say that the wind would not blow out of their mouths before it reached the other rider.

Behind them, the ridge held. Once, Veera looked back and saw a column of yellow smoke rising from one of the petroleum scouts. She did not know what it meant. She did not slow.

The clay tile was warm in her pocket against the carved horse, two small weights from two different peoples, both of them trusting her to carry their truth back to a wall that was still arguing about whether the truth applied to them.

The compound rose out of the southern haze faster than she had expected. The northern watch saw them coming and the horn sounded again, two short notes this time, the call for riders returning. The gates opened.

Veera rode through them at full speed, the sail spilling wind only at the last moment, and brought her bike to a stop in the same courtyard where she had been welcomed three days ago as a Worker carrying cargo.

Kessa was already there. Ulla was already there. Half the Council. The gene-reader at the back, his disc steady against his coat. Behind them the courtyard had filled without anyone calling it to: apprentices with tools still in their hands, wall-riders leading their bikes, the gatekeeper on her stair.

Veera swung down from the saddle and stood in the middle of the courtyard with the clay tile in one hand and the carved horse in the other.

“Twelve machines,” she said. “A fuel tank. Scouts that have already seen me. They are not raiding. They are coming to stay.”

Silence in the courtyard.

“And the Stone-Walkers,” she added, holding up the clay tile, “ride with us.”

The gene-reader’s disc began to pulse, faster than Veera had ever seen it move. Ulla stepped forward. Kessa stepped forward. For a long moment, neither of them spoke, because neither of them had yet decided which Council they belonged to.

Then the horn sounded again, three notes this time, and from the northern wall came the cry of a sentry seeing what he had been told to watch for.

The yellow smoke had reached the horizon visible from the compound.

Chapter 7

The Council Cracks

The council chamber was built to hold disagreement. Its walls were thick stone and its ceiling was high enough that no voice, no matter how raised, could fill it. A long oak table ran the length of the room, scarred by generations of decisions that had been argued across its surface, and seven chairs sat along it, one for each Forgemaster.

Beyond the door, boots and wheels and the scrape of stone being piled ran on against the northern wall; the chamber’s thick walls thinned it to weather. Lamps burned in iron brackets down both long walls, and their light moved on the table’s scars like water.

Six of them were occupied.

Kessa stood at the head of the table because she had refused to sit. Ulla sat at her right hand, her flat slate eyes unreadable in the lamplight. Across from Ulla, the gene-reader stood with his back to the wall and his disc steady against his coat, and beside him a man named Forgemaster Tibor, who Veera had not seen in the workshops because his work was elsewhere. Tibor was Pattern-Keeper of the compound, the Forgemaster whose hands tended the pattern-stones in the archive rather than the steel in the forge, whose memory carried the dimensions of every tool the Honor Society had ever recorded. He had read more of the old knowledge than any other living Forgemaster. Three more sat around the table. Two of them women, one of them older than Tlacaelel, with hair the color of old steel and a posture that had not bent in fifty years.

Veera and Sable stood at the foot of the table. They had not been invited to sit. They had not been told to leave either.

On the table between them sat two things. The clay tile, with its geometric markings turned up toward the light. And, beside it, a small map of the compound and its surrounding lands, weighted at the corners by pieces of dark iron.

They had been arguing for an hour.

* * *

“The pipeline routes are forty kilometers north,” Tibor was saying. “If the convoy holds its current speed, it reaches our outer fields by dawn. If it holds a slower speed, two days at most. Either way, we do not have time to summon allies.”

“Then we hold the wall,” said one of the women. “The compound has held against Scavenger raids for three generations. The walls were built to turn back attack.”

“The walls were built to turn back attack from bicycles and stilts and slings,” Tibor said. “Not from machines that can break stone.”

“We do not know that they can break stone.”

“We do not know that they cannot.”

Kessa held up a hand. The room quieted. She had not raised her voice once in the hour Veera had been standing there.

“The Worker rode to the convoy. She returned. She has seen what the rest of us have only smelled on the wind. I would like her to speak.”

Ulla’s slate eyes shifted, but she did not protest. The gene-reader’s disc pulsed once and was still. The older woman with the steel hair turned in her chair to look at Veera for the first time.

Veera felt every eye in the room settle on her. Her hand had gone to the carved horse in her pocket before she thought to send it there. She did not take it out. The weight of it against her fingers was steadying enough on its own.

“There are twelve large machines,” she said. Her voice came out steadier than she expected. “They are larger than the Scavenger fortress carts I have seen in pictures. They ride on wheels that are not made of any wood or steel I recognize, soft-looking but hard, and they leave deep cuts in the earth as they pass. Around them ride smaller machines, two-wheeled, like bicycles but driven by the same combustion. The riders wear coats of dark cloth and hard helmets that I could not see through. At the center of the procession, there is a tank.”

“A tank,” the gene-reader said, his voice neutral.

“A great cylinder of metal on its own wheels. It is full of what feeds them. They carry their own supply. They are not stopping to find more.”

“How do you know it is full?” Ulla asked. The question was not hostile.

“It rides low on its wheels. Heavy. The smaller machines drink from it without it growing lighter that I could see. And if it were empty, they would not bring it.”

A silence. The older woman with the steel hair, who was the compound’s Stone-Reader and had spent fifty years among the pattern-stones, nodded once, slowly.

“And the Stone-Walker tile,” Tibor said.

“Tlacaelel of the high places gave it to me. He said the Stone-Walkers have watched the petroleum gather for many seasons. They have ridden today because what comes south does not stop when bicycles do. They will buy us time on the ridges. They will not fight on the plain.”

“That they will fight at all,” the steel-haired woman said softly, “is more than this Council has any right to expect.”

* * *

Ulla rose.

She did not move to the head of the table. She moved to the foot, where Veera and Sable stood, and she stopped two paces from Veera. The slate eyes were still unreadable, but something in her posture had shifted, the way Sable’s eyes had shifted on the ridge.

“You rode out,” Ulla said, “without authorization. You took intelligence-gathering into your own hands. You returned with a Stone-Walker tile and a claim that the high peoples have allied themselves to us.”

“Yes.”

“You understand that this is the work of a scout. Not a trial-candidate. Not a Worker. A scout. Trained in the reading of distances and the assessment of threat.”

“I understand it now.”

Ulla looked at her for a long moment. Then she turned to the table.

“I have argued this morning that the criteria of the Trial of Understanding exist for reasons. I have argued that the Honor Society does not survive by improvising. I have argued that a Worker who passes by anomaly is not a Worker who has passed.”

She did not look at Kessa. Kessa did not look at her.

“I was wrong about the trial. I will not be wrong about this. The Worker rode where our scouts were too slow to ride. She returned with what we needed. If we hold to the rule that says only those of certain blood may carry our intelligence, we will die holding to it. I move that Veera ride as a sworn scout of the Honor Society, effective now, with the rights and access that designation carries.”

The room did not erupt. The room held very still.

Then Tibor said, “Seconded.”

The steel-haired woman raised one hand without speaking. So did two others. Kessa did not vote. She did not need to.

The gene-reader was the last. His disc had been pulsing all this time, steady and slow. He did not raise his hand. He did not lower it. He looked at Veera, and Veera looked back, and for the first time the disc against his coat did not seem to be reading her so much as recording what it could not measure.

“The vote carries,” he said quietly. “Whether I approve or not. Let it be entered that I have noted my reservations.”

“Noted,” Kessa said.

Above the chamber, boots crossed the wall-walk in their unhurried watch-rhythm. Down the table, the steel-haired woman lowered her raised hand, and pressed it flat to the oak for a moment before drawing it back.

* * *

They moved to the map.

Tibor laid out the lines of the convoy’s advance, the points where the pipeline routes crossed the old trade roads, the places where the plain narrowed and a machine the size of a small house would have to slow. The steel-haired woman, whose name was Marda, marked the ridges Tlacaelel and his people might hold. Sable named the draws and dips that a fast rider could use to flank without being seen, and Ulla listened to her without protest.

Kessa stood apart from them, her hands flat on the table. The lamps had burned down far enough by now that the iron corner-weights threw long shadows across the drawn ridges. Veera watched Kessa, and for the first time since the gene-reader had walked into the trial workshop, she saw something in Kessa’s face that looked like worry.

“What is it?” Veera asked, quietly enough that only Kessa heard.

“Six Forgemasters voted with me today. There is a seventh chair, and it is empty.”

Veera looked at the empty chair. It sat at the far end of the table, slightly apart from the others, its back darkened with age.

“Where is the seventh Forgemaster?”

“Forgemaster Vance has not been in the compound for three weeks. He left to inspect the northern outposts. He has not returned. He has not sent word.”

Kessa did not look at Veera when she said it. She was looking at the empty chair.

“I have not told the Council this, because I do not yet have proof. But I will tell you, because you will ride north again before this is over, and you should know what you may meet.”

She paused. Her hands did not move on the map.

“Vance has spent the last two years in the pattern-stone library. Tibor will tell you the same. He had Tibor’s permission to study the oldest stones, the ones from before our compound was built, the ones that carry knowledge we agreed long ago not to use. We do not destroy those stones. We keep them, because to lose a pattern is to lose what we may someday need to recognize. But we do not read from them. Vance read from them. He read for two years.”

She paused again. Her voice had not changed, but the silence around it had thickened.

“I do not believe Vance built the convoy. I do not believe Vance commands what is coming. He was a quiet man, a scholar, more comfortable with stones than with people. But the only person in the Honor Society who could have read those stones and understood what they said is Vance. And the only people who could have used what he read are people who knew he was reading. The pattern-stone library is in the inner archive. It is not a public place. Only Forgemasters enter it. And the gene-readers who certify the archive against rot and damp.”

Veera did not turn her head. She did not need to. She could feel the gene-reader’s disc on the far side of the room, steady against his coat, neither faster nor slower than it had been all evening.

“You think Vance,” Veera said, and could not make herself finish it.

“I think someone in our walls helped wake what should have stayed asleep. I do not know who. I do not know why. I know only that twelve machines do not appear on the horizon by themselves, that the seventh chair has been empty for three weeks, and that the patterns Vance was reading are not patterns a man can use alone.”

Sable, who had heard, said nothing. But Veera saw her hand drift to the tire-iron at her hip and rest there.

* * *

They dispersed before midnight. The plan was rough but it was a plan. Sable and Veera would ride at dawn to meet the Stone-Walkers on the eastern ridge. Tibor would lead a squad of Honor Society riders to the western draw. Marda would hold the compound with two-thirds of the trained crafters, ready to ride out if the wall was breached. The remaining Forgemasters would each take a section of the wall and a portion of the apprentices.

No one spoke of Vance. Kessa had asked Veera not to, and Veera had not.

She walked out of the council chamber into a courtyard that smelled of petroleum even here, the wind carrying it through the open gate. The stone-piling crews were still at the northern wall by torchlight, and the sound of their work ran on into the dark, stone on stone, without rhythm. Orin was waiting by the trial workshop door, his frame leaned against the wall beside him, polished still but no longer untested.

“They voted you in,” he said.

“They did.”

“And you will ride out again tomorrow.”

“I will.”

Orin nodded once, the same single nod he had given her over the gusset and over the chain-tension gauge.

“I am riding with the western squad. Tibor asked me. I said yes before I had decided whether I was afraid.”

Veera almost smiled. She did not quite manage it, but she felt the shape of it move through her face.

“That is how riding works,” she said. “You decide, and then you find out what it cost.”

They stood in the courtyard for a moment longer. The yellow smoke on the horizon was barely visible now in the dark, but the smell was thick enough that Veera could taste it. Somewhere on the northern wall, a sentry walked his round and his bootsteps were the only human sound for a long minute.

“Veera,” Orin said.

“Yes.”

“Whatever happens tomorrow, I am glad I was wrong about you.”

She looked at him in the dark, and his face was just a shape against the lamp-light spilling from the chamber door, but she could see the bright eyes from earlier in the day, the ones he had not let spill over. He was not letting them spill over now either. But they were bright.

“Goodnight, Orin.”

“Goodnight, Veera.”

She walked to the workshop, where her bike waited under the single oil lamp, the carved horse still riding the saddle where she had set it the night before. She lay down on her blanket without taking off her riding leathers. She did not sleep for a long time.

Outside the compound walls, the petroleum convoy moved south through the dark. On the high ridges to the north, Tlacaelel of the Stone-Walkers stood watch in the cold. And somewhere between the two, in a place no one had yet named, the seventh chair sat empty.

Chapter 8

The Eastern Ridge

Dawn came thick.

The smoke that had reached the horizon by nightfall had risen during the dark hours, and by the time Veera rode out through the eastern gate the sun was a dim coin behind a yellowish haze that smeared the whole sky. The plain looked wrong under that light. The grass was the wrong color. The shadows did not fall where they should have fallen.

Sable rode at her left, her multi-gear humming, her tire-iron strapped to her saddle within reach of her right hand. Behind them, six other riders from the Honor Society, all young, all selected by Ulla for the lightness of their frames and the speed of their hands. Veera did not know their names. She would learn them on the ride or she would not learn them at all.

The carved horse was in her pocket. The clay tile was in her other pocket. Her sail was furled tight against the rear mast, because the wind today was wrong for riding under canvas, gusting in eddies from too many directions at once. She would unfurl it when the ridge gave her the shape of the air. Not before.

* * *

They rode for two hours without speaking.

The compound had drilled them in formation before they left, but a formation drilled on a courtyard did not survive contact with the prairie. Within the first kilometer the riders had spread into a loose wedge, Sable on the left flank, Veera at the point, the six Honor Society riders staggered behind in the way that Sable had quietly insisted on against the formation Ulla had ordered. Sable had said only, “The formation Ulla teaches is for parade. The one I am giving you is for surviving.”

Ulla had not argued.

They followed a draw that ran east-northeast toward the long ridge Tlacaelel had named. The earth here was harder, the grass shorter, the wind colder. Veera had ridden this terrain only once before, hauling oil to an outpost that had since been abandoned. She did not remember it being this quiet.

No birds. That was what was wrong. The plain had been emptied of birds.

* * *

They climbed the ridge by the south face, where the slope was gentle enough for a bicycle, and crested it just before the sun would have been overhead if there had been a sun to see.

The Stone-Walkers were already there.

Not six of them this time. Thirty. Maybe forty. They lined the ridge in a long row, their stilts planted in the hard earth, their bows held loose at their sides, their painted faces turned south to watch what came toward them across the haze. Among them stood three of their walking-houses, not the great war-towers of the Plain of Memories raid but smaller, swifter structures built for ridges and broken ground, walking-workshops adapted for war. Their banners did not move. The wind did not reach this high.

Tlacaelel saw them coming and descended the line to meet them.

“You have brought a small army,” he said, looking at the six Honor Society riders.

“They are scouts,” Veera said. “Not warriors. Not yet.”

“They are warriors if they ride here today. The plain decides what people are. Not the Council.”

He gestured toward the line of Stone-Walkers on the ridge.

“We have called those who would come. There are more on the western peaks, and more again to the north of where you saw the convoy. We are not many. But we hold high ground, and on the high ground we are enough to make them choose.”

“Choose what?”

“Whether to spend their machines climbing for us, or whether to leave the ridges to us and roll on through the plain to your wall. They cannot do both.”

Veera looked south. From the ridge, the convoy was now clearly visible, perhaps eight kilometers out, its yellow smoke rising in twelve distinct columns that wavered and recombined in the haze. The smaller two-wheeled machines ranged ahead of the main body in pairs, casting wide.

Scouts. Looking for them.

“They know we are here,” Sable said quietly. “They have known since yesterday. They are choosing whether to spend their machines or not.”

“Yes,” Tlacaelel said. “And we will make the choice expensive for them.”

* * *

It began with two scouts.

They came at the ridge from the southwest, the way a scavenging dog comes at a thing it has not yet decided is food. Their machines climbed slowly on the south face, the soft black wheels biting into the loose earth and throwing it backward in twin plumes. The riders crouched low over their handlebars, dark coats flapping, helmets like blank insect heads.

The Stone-Walkers waited until the machines were two-thirds up the slope. Then six bows drew at once, and six arrows flew, and one of the machines lost its rider before the rider had heard the bowstrings.

The other scout turned to run. He did not turn fast enough. A second volley caught him at the shoulder of his coat and the machine cartwheeled down the slope, spilling fuel that caught the sparks of its own dying engine and bloomed into a yellow fire that climbed the air for a full breath before it sank to a smear of black smoke.

Veera had seen people die before. She had seen Workers go down under Stone-Walker harpoons on the Plain of Memories. But she had not seen anything burn the way the petroleum burned, with that hungry yellow tongue and that smell that coated the inside of her mouth. She turned her head and spat on the ridge stone. Beside her, the youngest of the six riders, a boy who had split his lip on the cold of the morning ride, watched the fire until it sank, and did not spit, and did not look away either.

“Now they know,” Tlacaelel said.

“Know what?”

“The shape of the cost.”

* * *

What came next was not scouts.

From the main body of the convoy, three of the large machines peeled away and began to climb the ridge in formation, their engines roaring loud enough that Veera felt it in her chest from a kilometer away. They moved slower than the scouts but they moved steadier, their wheels gripping where the scouts’ had slipped, their flanks armored in thick plates that the Stone-Walker arrows would not pierce.

“They have sent the big ones,” Sable said.

“Yes.” Tlacaelel watched them climb without alarm. “This is why we built the houses smaller this season.”

He gave a signal. The three Stone-Walker walking-houses on the ridge unfolded.

Veera had not understood, until that moment, what the Stone-Walkers had been building since the day she drove her cargo transport into Tlacaelel’s stilts on the Plain of Memories. The walking-houses on the ridge were not houses. They were weapons. The same cable-and-counterweight knowledge that lifted a walking-house onto its legs, the same pulley systems the Stone-Walkers used to suspend a workpiece in midair for forging, had been turned outward. Each smaller house folded into a long, low arm of jointed steel that could lower itself down the slope and reach the climbing machines with the kind of leverage no bow could match. At the end of each arm hung a great hooked blade of hammered iron, the size of a Worker’s shield, swinging slowly in the haze.

“Stone-cutter,” Tlacaelel said, the same wind-chime laugh in his voice. “We have used them on rock-walls for many years. We thought perhaps they would also work on petroleum dragons.”

The first stone-cutter swung. It missed the lead machine by a meter, but the second swing did not miss. The hooked blade caught the side of the machine just below its armored flank and the leverage of the long arm did what no arrow could do: it tore. Metal screamed. Yellow fuel sprayed in an arc. The machine swerved, lost its line on the slope, and rolled.

The other two machines stopped climbing.

They sat there on the slope, engines idling, the riders inside them invisible behind their thick windows, calculating something. Veera could feel the calculation.

“They will choose now,” Tlacaelel said. “Whether the ridge is worth what it will cost.”

They chose.

The two machines reversed slowly down the slope, found the level ground at its base, and rejoined the main convoy. The convoy did not slow. It did not turn. It kept rolling south, at the same indifferent pace, leaving the ridge to the Stone-Walkers and continuing toward the compound on the plain.

On the ridge, no one sang. The stone-cutter arms hung where their last swings had left them, the hooked blades turning slowly on their cables, and along the line the painted faces watched the smoke columns shrink toward the south. Under Veera’s boots the ground-hum thinned. It was not dying. It was leaving, carrying itself toward the wall.

* * *

Veera understood, looking down at the convoy, that they had won the ridge and lost everything else.

The Stone-Walkers had done what they had promised. They had made the high ground expensive. They had made the petroleum people choose. And the petroleum people had chosen to leave them alone and ride for the wall.

“They will reach the compound by noon tomorrow,” Sable said. “Maybe sooner.”

“And the wall will hold them for how long?”

Sable did not answer. She did not need to.

Tlacaelel was watching Veera. He had not spoken for several minutes. The wind on the ridge was finally moving, and it was moving in a direction Veera knew.

“Veera-Who-Rides-Steel-Horses,” he said. “There is a thing I did not tell you yesterday, because yesterday you did not yet have the standing to hear it.”

“Tell me now.”

“The convoy did not come from nowhere. It came from a place. A place where the petroleum was woken. A place where the old wells have been opened and the old machines have been fed. The Stone-Walkers know where it is, because we have watched its smoke for a season. We have not gone there because we are few, and the place is many. But if a rider were to go there now, while the convoy is far from home, while the place is thin of defenders, that rider might find what is feeding the machines. And what feeds them can be taken from them.”

Veera looked north. Beyond the ridge, beyond the haze, the plain rose into something the Honor Society maps did not name, something her grandmother had only spoken of in the lowest voice she had ever used.

“You want me to ride to the wells.”

“I want you to choose. The convoy moves toward your wall. Your wall will fight, or your wall will fall. Either way, the petroleum will keep coming until someone closes the wells. I cannot ask you to ride away from your people in their hour. But I can tell you that riding toward them will save them for one day. Riding away from them, in this direction, might save them for many.”

He pressed a second clay tile into her hand. Larger than the first. Marked with a different pattern, a long spiral that wound inward to a single point.

“The map. Those who can read it will read it. Those who cannot will at least know that you did not ride alone.”

* * *

Veera stood on the ridge with two clay tiles in her hands and the carved horse in her pocket and the wind finally moving in a direction she understood.

South, to the wall, where Kessa and Orin and Ulla and Marda and Tibor would meet the convoy with whatever they had built and trained for. Where her testimony of what she had seen would steady the riders who had never seen petroleum burn. Where Orin’s polished frame would take its first true ride and find out what it was made of.

Or north, into a country no Honor Society rider had ever entered, toward a place her grandmother had only named once, to find out whether the petroleum could be cut off at its root.

Sable, beside her, was watching the convoy roll south through the haze. She had not asked Veera what she would choose. She would not. She had said it on the ride out: I am telling you what is needed. What you do with that is yours.

Veera turned to her.

“I cannot ride both.”

“No.”

“But you can.”

Sable did not answer immediately. Her eyes moved from Veera to the six Honor Society riders gathered behind them, who had been quiet through the engagement and were quieter now, watching the smoke from the burning machine drift up the slope. They were young. They had seen petroleum burn. Some of them would not have ridden out at all if Sable had not been with them in the formation she had named for surviving.

“You want me to ride them home,” Sable said. It was not quite a question.

“I want you to ride them home. To tell Kessa what we saw. To stand on the wall when the convoy reaches it and steady the ones who have never seen what we have seen. To make sure that what Ulla voted for last night is honored when the smoke rolls in.”

Sable looked at the riders again. Then at Veera. Then at the ridge that ran north out of the haze toward a country neither of them had ever ridden.

“You will ride alone.”

“Tlacaelel’s people watch the high places. I will not be alone for long.”

Sable’s mouth did not move, but something in her eyes shifted the way it had shifted in the courtyard the day before, the way still water shifts when the wind first touches it. She did not argue.

“Three days,” Sable said. “After three days, I will come for you. With whoever I can bring. Whether the wall holds or not.”

“Three days.”

Tlacaelel, who had been listening, lifted one hand in the Stone-Walker sign of acknowledgment.

“The high places will hold the corridor. A rider going north today rides under our bows. A rider going south today rides under no one’s. Choose your roads with this knowledge.”

Veera handed Sable the first clay tile, the one Tlacaelel had given her at the convoy. She kept the spiral tile, the map, in her own hand.

“Show this to Kessa. Tell her the Stone-Walkers ride with us. Tell her where I have gone.”

“And if you do not come back in three days?”

“Then I have failed. And the wall has to be enough.”

Sable took the tile. Her fingers closed around it for a moment longer than was necessary.

“Ride well, Veera.”

“Ride well, Sable.”

They did not embrace. Sable did not embrace anyone, and Veera had learned by now not to ask. But Sable swung onto her multi-gear and gathered the six riders behind her with a single glance, and they peeled off the ridge in the loose wedge formation she had taught them, riding south fast, downslope into the thickening haze of the afternoon, riding for the wall.

Veera watched them go until the haze took them.

Then she turned her bike north. The wind was at her back. The sail filled. The spiral tile sat warm against her hip from the long ride, and the carved horse against the other, two small weights from two different peoples, both of them riding with her now into a country that had no name on any Honor Society map.

She did not look back. Looking back was for riders who had not yet decided.

Chapter 9

What the Wall Held

The horn sounded a different note when Sable led the riders through the gate.

Two notes for returning riders. Three notes for a returning rider who had seen the enemy. Sable had ridden the last half-kilometer at full sprint to make sure the watch had time to count them, six riders on six bikes, no Worker among them, no sail rig above the rear wheels. The sentry on the north tower had not understood what he was being told until Sable was already inside the courtyard. By then Kessa was running.

“Where is she?”

“Riding north. Alone. Under Stone-Walker cover for as far as their ridges hold.”

Kessa did not stop running. She wheeled toward the council chamber without breaking pace, her hands already moving in the small gestures she used when ordering apprentices, and Sable swung down from her bike and followed. Behind them, the six Honor Society riders sat slumped on their saddles, suddenly without orders, the readiness that had carried them up and down the ridge now draining out of them, leaving their hands loose and clumsy on the bars. Apprentices came forward with water. The riders drank without looking at each other. One of them, a young man with a cracked lip, was shaking in a way he probably thought he was hiding.

The petroleum smoke on the horizon was closer now. Close enough that the wall sentries no longer had to look hard for it. Close enough that the compound smelled of it the way a forge smelled of iron, constantly, until the nose forgot.

* * *

Sable laid the clay tile on the council table and Kessa read it the way the Stone-Walkers had said only those who could read it would.

“High places held,” she said. “Corridor open for Veera. The convoy will reach our wall before noon tomorrow.”

“Veera rode north on the spiral map,” Sable said. “Toward the wellhead. Three days.”

Tibor closed his eyes. Ulla did not.

“She will not survive three days alone in that country,” Ulla said. The slate eyes were not unreadable now. “No matter what the high places hold. The convoy has scouts behind the lines, supply runners, machines we have not seen. She is one rider on one bike with one sail.”

“Then we send a second rider,” Tibor said.

“We have no second rider faster than what is already on the road.” Ulla’s mouth tightened. “Veera has a half-day’s lead and a tailwind. Anything we send now would not catch her before nightfall, and the wellhead is two days further than that.”

Kessa was looking at the empty seventh chair.

“A racing bike could catch her,” she said quietly. “An Honor Society racing bike, ridden by someone who has not been riding cargo or scouting ridges. Someone whose frame is fresh.”

Sable, who had been standing at the edge of the table, looked up.

“Orin,” she said.

“Orin.”

Ulla’s slate eyes moved from Kessa to Sable to the empty chair, and back to Kessa. She did not speak for a long moment.

“He is on the western squad. Tibor has him in formation for tomorrow.”

“Tibor can find another rider for the western squad.” Kessa met Ulla’s eyes. “He cannot find another rider for the road Veera is on.”

Ulla closed her eyes. When she opened them, she looked tired in a way Veera would not have recognized, but Sable did.

“Send him,” Ulla said. “And send him now. The wall will hold what it holds without him.”

* * *

The horn sounded again before Sable left the chamber. Four notes this time, the call she had been hoping not to hear before nightfall.

The convoy was at the outer fields.

Kessa moved without speaking. Tibor followed. Ulla was already half a step ahead of them, her riding leathers cinched, her hair tied back. Sable went to find Orin.

The compound had been in motion all day, but this was a different motion. Apprentices ran with bundles of arrows. The Pattern-Keeper’s assistants carried pattern-leathers down from the archive to be used as field references for emergency repairs. Marda the Stone-Reader stood at the gate of the inner archive with a key in her hand that Sable had never seen before, and her face was the face of someone who had decided what she would burn if the wall fell.

The yellow smoke had reached the outer fields. The first machine came over the low rise where the cargo route met the trade road, and the second behind it, and the third, and the fourth, and they came on without slowing, and the wall sentries fired their first arrows knowing the arrows would do nothing because they had to fire something.

Sable did not stop to watch. She crossed the courtyard at a run.

* * *

The western wall was the quiet one. The compound had been built in two rings, an outer wall broken by four gates at the cardinal points and an inner wall that closed around the forge-yard like a second skin, and the convoy had thrown all its weight at the eastern gate and left the other three sides to wait. Each Forgemaster held a section of the outer ring. The western belonged to Tibor, and Tibor had put Orin on it. From the parapet, a chest-high breastwork of fitted stone that a rider could crouch behind, the outer fields ran west and empty into the failing light, pale and undefended, nothing in them yet trying to kill anyone. Behind Sable, over her shoulder, the smoke of the eastern fight climbed into a sky already the wrong color. That was what the western wall overlooked: nothing, for now.

Orin was on it, where Tibor had stationed him, his racing bike leaned against the parapet beside him because there was no good reason to sit a racing bike while the enemy came at a wall.

He saw Sable coming. He set down his bow.

“You came back,” he said.

“I came back. She did not.”

She told him. The ridge. The stone-cutters. The convoy choosing to roll on. The second tile. The spiral map. The three days. Orin listened without interrupting.

When Sable finished, he looked at the western horizon, where the smoke was still thin because the convoy was hitting the eastern gate, not this one.

“You are telling me to go after her.”

“I am telling you what I told her on the ride out. I am telling you what is needed. What you do with that is yours.”

Orin’s hand was on the parapet stone. He did not move it for a long moment. Sable did not push him. She had not pushed Veera. She had learned long ago that a rider chose her road, or his, and the only useful question was whether the rider would have what she needed for the choosing.

“I am not the rider you want for this.” Orin’s voice was steady, but not in the way an Honor Society voice was usually steady. “I have ridden one trial in my life. I have never ridden alone past the eastern outposts. I do not know the country she is in. I do not know how to read a Stone-Walker map. I do not know what I would do if I caught her.”

“I know.”

“Then why me?”

Sable leaned against the parapet beside him. The first volley of arrows had gone up from the eastern wall by now, and the first crash of a machine hitting the gate had sounded, but neither of them looked east. There would be time for the wall in a moment. There was not time for what Sable had to say to wait until after the wall.

“When I watched you on the ridge yesterday, the way you rode the climb,” she said, “I saw what Veera saw on the testing ground. The three seconds when your hands forgot what they had been told. The body knowing what the mind had not given it permission to know. I have seen that look on exactly two other riders in my life. One of them is Veera. The other is dead now, and she died because she rode alone, and there was no one beside her who had also forgotten what their hands had been told.”

Orin did not move. The smoke from the eastern wall had begun to drift over them.

“Veera does not need the rider you think you should be. She needs the rider you became in that workshop. The one who corrected his own down-tube angle by feel. The one who handed her his chain-tension gauge without saying why. The one who built a frame that passed because it had to pass, and stood there with bright eyes while Kessa said the words. That rider has work to do, Orin. And that work is not at this wall.”

A long silence. The eastern wall was loud now. The western wall, where they stood, was still quiet, because the convoy had committed its first wave to one side of the compound and had not yet remembered the other.

“And if the wall falls while I am riding north?”

“Then we will have failed at the wall, and you will be the only Honor Society rider still moving. You will reach her, and the two of you will close the wells, or you will not, and either way the only thing that will have changed is whether the petroleum keeps coming.”

Sable straightened from the parapet. She held out her hand. In it was a small folded square of cured leather, marked in the same compact script Veera had seen on her workshop visits.

“Scavenger pattern-leather. Reads like a Stone-Walker map if you know how, and you do not, but the spiral on it matches the one Veera carries. You follow the spiral inward. You stop when the spiral stops. You will find her, or you will find what she found.”

Orin took the leather. He held it in his hand for a moment without opening it. Then he folded it small and put it in the inner pocket of his riding coat, the one over his ribs, where the Honor Society kept the prayers their mothers wrote for them when they left on long rides.

“Sable.”

“Yes.”

“Tell Tibor I am sorry for the western squad.”

“Tibor knows.”

Orin lifted his racing bike from the parapet. The frame caught the late afternoon light the way it had caught the morning light on the testing ground, every joint mitered to a tolerance that had taken him years to reach. He swung onto the saddle and tested the chain tension with one hand, and the gauge was not in its usual pocket because it was with Veera. He smiled, very briefly, at the absence.

Then he rode for the western gate, which had not yet been engaged, and the gatekeeper opened it for him without asking why because Sable had run ahead and told her. The racing bike made almost no sound going, its wheels trued to silence, and the gate swallowed what little there was.

Orin rode out under a sky that was the wrong color, into a country that was not his, on a bike that had been built for racing and would now be asked to do something else, carrying a Scavenger pattern-leather over his ribs and an Honor Society’s training in his hands and a name he had said for the first time without precision:

Veera. I am coming.

* * *

The wall held.

Not easily. Not without cost. The eastern gate took two strikes from a large machine before the bracing buckled inward and the third strike broke a hinge that Tibor’s people had reinforced overnight. Kessa called the eastern position back inside the second wall and let the convoy spend its forward momentum on a courtyard that had been cleared and trapped and arranged for exactly this. Three of the petroleum scouts were killed inside the trap. One large machine caught fire from a thrown lamp of plant oil that ignited its own fuel and burned in the courtyard for half an hour, the smoke so thick that the apprentices manning the second wall had to fight in masks of wet cloth.

Two Honor Society riders died at the eastern gate. The cracked-lip boy from Sable’s ridge squad was one of them. He had asked for a place on the wall and Ulla had given him one and he had held it for forty minutes before a scout’s projectile took him below the ribs.

By sundown the convoy had pulled back to the edge of the outer fields and was regrouping. The compound was still standing. The second wall was still whole. The forge fires had not gone out. But everyone who had been on the eastern wall knew, and everyone who had not been there but had smelled the petroleum burning, that the convoy had not committed its full strength to the first attack. It had been measuring. It had been learning what the wall held.

Tomorrow it would come with what it had learned.

* * *

Sable stood on the western parapet after the horn sounded the all-clear and watched the road Orin had ridden.

It was a road she had ridden once, long ago, when she had been the rider who left and someone else had been the rider who stayed, and the stayer had not survived the staying. She had not spoken of it in twenty years. She had spoken of it today, for the first time, because the gene-reader’s disc had been pulsing in the council chamber all evening at the same steady rate, and Sable had decided that some things were worth being said out loud while the saying still mattered.

The yellow smoke on the eastern horizon had thinned but had not gone. The petroleum people were still out there, still drinking from their walking oil-raven, still measuring what the wall held.

And somewhere in the country north of the ridges, two riders rode toward a place that had no name on any Honor Society map. One of them had ridden out under wind that was finally moving in her favor. The other had ridden out under wind that did not yet know which way it would turn.

Sable hoped the wind would hold.

Chapter 10

The Country With No Name

The country changed before Veera noticed she had crossed into it.

She had been riding north for three hours under the Stone-Walker corridor, the ridges on either side of her holding their long quiet watch, the painted faces of warriors appearing now and again on a high outcrop and acknowledging her with a single lifted hand before vanishing back into the rock. The wind held. The sail filled. The bike answered. She was making better time than she had expected to make, and she had begun to allow herself the small mistake of believing the country knew her.

Then the grass changed.

It came on gradually, the way a fever came on. The prairie grass that had carried her bike since the eastern gate began to thin, then to gray, then to give way to a different growth altogether, low and pale and bristled, the color of bone left in the sun. The earth beneath it went hard. Her wheels began to ring on it instead of hum. She passed a small dry watercourse and the bed was lined with crystals that caught the light and did not look like any salt she had seen on a cargo run.

She slowed. She did not stop. Her grandmother had taught her that a rider who slowed in unfamiliar country lived longer than one who stopped.

* * *

The first sign was the shadow.

It crossed the ground in front of her bike, long and narrow and moving faster than any cloud-shadow she had ever seen. By the time she looked up, the sky was empty. She rode another fifty meters and the shadow came again, from a different angle this time, and again she looked up and saw nothing.

She slowed further. She furled the sail. Without the wind in her canvas, the bike grew quiet, and in the quiet she heard it.

A sound she had no word for. A kind of singing, but not the singing the wheels made on the open prairie, not the song of spokes turning at speed. It was higher than that. Thinner. It came from above her, from several directions at once, the way a flock of birds gave a single sound that seemed to come from everywhere because the birds were everywhere.

But there were no birds. There had not been any birds for two days.

She thought, very clearly, of her grandmother saying:

When you hear a sound in the sky that you cannot place, do not look for it. The looking is what they want. Find cover and wait until the singing stops.

Veera looked for cover.

There was a low scarp twenty paces to her left, a shelf where the ground had broken and dropped a body’s height, leaving a lip of weathered gray stone with a hollow scooped under it by some old water that no longer ran. It was rock, not metal, the first honest stone she had seen since the grass turned, and the hollow beneath the lip would hide a bike and a rider who lay flat. She rode for it without speeding, because speeding made noise, and brought her bike behind the scarp and stepped off the saddle and laid the frame flat in the dust. Then she crouched against the stone and waited.

The singing passed overhead. Once. Then again. Then again, from a different direction.

On the fourth pass she risked a glance.

They were higher than she had thought. Five of them, perhaps six. They were difficult to count, moving in a loose formation that opened and closed as they rode the air. They were not birds. They were not any kind of bird that had ever existed. They had wings, but the wings were wrong, too long and too still, held out from their bodies in a fixed shape that did not flap. Beneath each wing hung a figure, a human figure, riding the wing the way a Worker rode a sail, with cords running from their hands to the wing’s edges.

Gliders.

The word came to her without her grandmother’s voice attached to it. Her grandmother had not used the word, but Veera had heard it once, in a story told by a passing trader who had come through her settlement when she was nine. People who flew. People who launched from mountain peaks and rode the wind for hours at a time. People who carried sounds with them, the trader had said, sounds that announced their coming, sounds that the lowland peoples had learned to associate with death.

She had not believed the trader. She had only been nine.

She believed him now.

The singing came from cords on the wings, she could see it now, resin-coated cords that the wind played as the wings rode the air. Each glider sang at a slightly different pitch. Together they made the sound she had heard, a sound that seemed to come from everywhere because it was coming from everywhere. She caught herself listening into it the way she listened into spokes, for the fray, for the flaw, for the mood, and finding none of these, only pitch on pitch, patient and clean.

They were not flying in formation toward anything in particular. They were searching. They were searching the way she had been searching when she rode the ridge yesterday and counted the convoy. They were counting something on the ground.

They were counting her.

Veera pressed her back against the scarp and made herself smaller. She had laid the bike flat with the sail furled and the metal of the frame mostly hidden in the bone-colored grass, and she had to believe that from above she was a shape the gliders could not yet name. Her riding leathers were the color of dried earth. Her hair was the dull brown of dried grass, a color nothing in this country would look at twice. If she did not move, perhaps.

The singing passed once more. Then it began to thin, the formation pulling apart and rising into the haze that hung over the northern plain, the figures growing smaller until they were less than birds, less than insects, less than nothing at all.

They were gone.

Veera did not move for a long time. Her hand had found the carved horse in her pocket without her noticing, and the wood was warm in a way she did not understand, warmer than her body or the cold day could account for. The spiral tile in her other pocket was warm too, but that was only the plain warmth of a thing carried against the skin. The horse was something else.

* * *

When she finally lifted the bike out of the dust and remounted, she was still trying to decide what she had just seen.

The Stone-Walker corridor was supposed to extend at least another half day’s ride north. Tlacaelel had been clear about it. The high places were held. A rider going north today rides under our bows.

But the high places where the gliders had launched from were not the ridges Tlacaelel held. They were further north. Beyond the corridor. In a country the Stone-Walkers themselves had spoken of only in warnings.

And the gliders had not fired on her. They had searched, and they had passed, and they had gone.

Either they had not seen her, which Veera was not arrogant enough to believe. Or they had seen her and passed on. What the passing bought her, she could not yet read.

Her grandmother had only one rule for a rider who could not tell whether the sky was a friend or an enemy.

Ride faster.

Veera unfurled the sail and rode.

* * *

She crossed out of the corridor in the late afternoon. She knew because the Stone-Walker waystation was the last marker of it, and she had reached it.

It was a small construction, no larger than a Worker’s hut, but it stood on four short legs above the bone-grass and its walls were the same woven plant-fiber as a walking-house, treated with resin that had darkened with seasons. A water cistern sat beside it. A pole rose from the roof with a single white pennant that did not move in the wind.

A Stone-Walker was waiting for her.

Not Tlacaelel. A woman, taller than Veera by a head, painted in a different pattern than Tlacaelel’s, her stilts shorter than the war-stilts because this was a watching station, not a fighting one. She lifted a hand in greeting as Veera approached. Veera did the same.

“You are the Worker who rides for the wells.”

“I am.”

“Tlacaelel sent word ahead by ridge-runner. I have water for your water-skin, food for your saddlebags, and a thing he wished you to know before you ride past the corridor.”

Veera dismounted. The woman watched the bike with the careful attention Stone-Walkers gave to wheeled things, the curiosity of a people who had chosen not to ride them and therefore studied them the way they would have studied a foreign animal.

“I saw flyers,” Veera said.

The woman’s face did not change, but her stilts shifted slightly under her, the way a rider shifted weight when a wind changed.

“How many?”

“Five. Maybe six. They sang.”

“And they did not fire on you.”

“No.”

The woman was quiet for a long moment. The white pennant on the pole above her stirred once, briefly, in a wind Veera could not feel.

“This is the thing Tlacaelel wished you to know. The Gliders have not chosen a side. They have flown above the petroleum convoy for many days, and they have not fired on it. They have flown above our ridges for many days, and they have not fired on us. They are watching. They are deciding. We do not know what they will decide. We do not know who they decide for.”

“They counted me.”

“Yes. They count everyone. The counting is how they decide, a weighing of every rider and walker and machine that passes beneath them, to learn who is strong and who is already finished. If you ride well, and they see you ride well, perhaps the counting will fall in your favor. If you ride badly, they will know that too. The Gliders do not fight for the losing side. They have learned this lesson over many seasons, and they teach it to each generation, and the lesson is the only thing that has kept them alive in the high places.”

Veera filled her water skin from the cistern. The water tasted of stone and clay. It tasted clean.

“How far to the wells from here?”

“Two days at your pace. One day if you do not sleep. The country between here and there is no longer ours. We do not ride past this waystation. The bone-grass thickens to a kind of small thorn-tree forest. There is water if you know where, and the spiral on your tile will name the places. Beyond the forest, the land flattens again, and then you will see the smoke.”

“And the Gliders?”

“They go where they wish. They watch what they choose. Ride well, Veera-Who-Rides-Steel-Horses. The high places will pray for the kind of riding that earns their count.”

* * *

Veera rode out of the waystation as the sun began to drop, and within an hour the bone-grass had given way to the thorn-tree forest the woman had described. The trees were waist-high, twisted, gray, their thorns the length of a finger. They tore at her riding leathers if she rode too close to them, and she learned quickly to read the gaps the spiral tile marked, gaps that were not gaps a casual rider would have found.

The country was no longer hers. The country was no longer anyone’s that she knew. But the carved horse warmed against her hip, and her bike held its line through the gaps, and her sail furled and unfurled as the wind came and went through the thorn-tree canopy.

She did not see the gliders again. But she felt them, or felt the possibility of them, the whole time. She rode with her ears open in a way she had not learned to ride before. She listened for the singing. The singing did not come.

When darkness fell, she found the small hollow the spiral named, a place where the thorn-trees opened around a flat stone that had clearly been used before by riders who knew the spiral. She laid her bike down and ate the dried fruit the Stone-Walker woman had given her, and she did not light a fire, because a fire in this country was a thing that would call attention from peoples she did not yet know how to read.

She lay on her back on the flat stone with the bike beside her and the carved horse on her chest and the spiral tile under her palm. Above her, the sky was the wrong color, even in the dark. The petroleum haze had reached this far. The stars she had been taught to navigate by were dim and few, and the ones she could see were not in the patterns her grandmother had named.

She thought of Sable riding the six home. She thought of Orin on the western wall. She thought of Kessa in the council chamber with the gene-reader’s disc steady against his coat. She thought of the wall, which would either hold or not hold, and which her riding would not affect tonight.

She thought of the wellhead. Two days away. Or one, if she did not sleep.

She slept anyway. Her grandmother had taught her that a rider who did not sleep made worse mistakes than a rider who slept badly, and Veera trusted the teaching.

She dreamed of horses.

Not the Sacred Horses of the old stories. A single horse, a real one, gray-brown and long-legged, running across a prairie that had no name. The horse looked at her once, the way a horse looked at a rider who had finally arrived, and then it ran on without her, and Veera ran behind it on her own two feet because she did not have a bike in the dream, and the running was easy, and the wind was at her back, and somewhere far ahead the horse slowed to wait.

She woke before dawn. The carved horse was warm on her chest.

She rode.

Chapter 11

The Second Rider

The thorn country did not want her in it.

By the second morning Veera had learned its grammar, the way she had once learned the grammar of the Plain of Memories, but this was a harder tongue. The thorn-trees grew close and low and gray, and the gaps the spiral tile named were not gaps she would have trusted on her own, narrow runs of packed grit that curved away from where the eye said the open ground lay. Twice she had ignored the tile and trusted her eyes, and twice the thorns had opened her leathers along the forearm and drawn a thin line of blood for the lesson. After that she rode the spiral and did not argue.

Above her, the sky held its wrong color, a yellow-brown haze that thickened toward the north until it was less a sky than a ceiling. There were no stars in it at night and no clean sun in it by day, only a dull coin of light that crossed the haze without warming anything under it. Her grandmother had taught her to navigate by star-roads and wind-paths. Neither of them worked here. She navigated by the spiral, and by the smell, which grew stronger the further north she rode, the dead earth-blood thick enough now that her enhanced lungs had stopped flinching at it and simply reported it, the way a nose forgot a forge.

She was being counted. She had known it since the waystation, and she had felt it since dawn.

The first one came just after the light cleared the haze. She heard it before she saw it, the thin resin-cord singing she had learned to fear on the open bone-grass, and she did not look up, because her grandmother’s rule held even here. The looking is what they want. She kept her line through the thorns and rode as if the singing were only wind moving in the canopy. It passed once, low enough that its shadow slid across her front wheel, and she felt the pressure of being seen the way she had felt the weight of the Council’s platform on the testing ground. Then it rose, and the singing thinned back into the haze, and Veera let out a breath she had not known she was holding.

Ride well. If you ride badly, they will know that too.

So she rode well. She rode the thorn-gaps clean, her body low and quiet over the asymmetric frame, her hands making their small corrections before her mind had named the reasons for them, and somewhere above the yellow ceiling she let herself believe that something was watching the way she rode and adding it to a sum she would never be shown.

* * *

She heard the second set of wheels near midday.

For one cold moment she thought the Gliders had come down out of the haze, or that the petroleum people had put riders into the thorns behind her. She brought the bike into the cover of a thorn-stand and drew the tire-iron and waited, and her heart did the thing her caste had bred it to do, flooding her hands with blood and readiness before she had decided to be ready.

Then the wheels resolved into a rhythm she knew. One gear, ridden too hard. A racing frame, its chain singing at a pitch that no Scavenger drivetrain and no cargo hauler had ever reached.

Orin came around the thorn-stand at a speed the country did not permit, saw her too late to stop clean, and laid the racing bike down in a long controlled slide that spared the frame and cost him a strip of skin along one forearm. He came up out of the dust already looking for her, and when he found her standing there with the tire-iron still half raised, something moved through his face that she had seen only once before, on the testing ground, in the moment after Kessa said the words.

“You are hard to catch,” he said.

The racing bike was ruined and it was beautiful. He had ridden a machine built for smooth prairie across two days of country that ate frames for sport, and it showed on every surface: the brazing scratched down to bare brass, one spoke gone from the rear wheel and the wheel still running because he had trued it around the gap by feel, the translucent racing sail torn and knotted and torn again. It was the most abused thing Veera had ever seen an Honor Society rider permit himself to own, and he had brought it to her across a country that was not his, on a road he could not read, chasing a spiral he could never have followed alone.

“Sable,” Veera said.

“Sable.” He straightened, one hand pressed to the bleeding arm without seeming to notice it was his. “She told me what she told you on the ride out. That she would tell me what was needed, and the rest was mine.” He almost smiled. “Then she put a piece of Scavenger leather in my hand and told me to follow the spiral inward and stop when it stopped.”

He drew the folded leather from the inner pocket of his coat, the one over his ribs, and held it out beside the clay tile in Veera’s palm. The two spirals were the same spiral. Stone-Walker clay and Scavenger hide, cut by peoples who had never sat at the same fire, both of them winding inward to the same single point.

“She said something else,” Orin went on. “About two riders. About what it costs to ride alone. She would not tell me who the other rider was.”

Veera said nothing. It was Sable’s to tell and not hers. She looked at Orin, at the wrecked and lovely bike, at the blood he had not noticed, at the plain fact of him being here at all, and she felt the carved horse warm in her pocket.

“You should not have brought a racing bike,” she said.

“It was the fastest thing in the compound.” He looked at the ruined frame with the particular grief of a rider watching his own self-portrait come apart. “It will not make the ride home.”

“Then we will build you something that will.”

He looked at her, and something passed between them that neither of them named, because there was a wellhead two days’ smell to the north and no hour in the day for the naming of it.

“How far?” he asked.

“The tile says we clear the thorns by dusk.” She swung back onto her saddle. “After that it says we stop guessing.”

* * *

They cleared the thorns by dusk, exactly as the spiral had promised, and climbed the long back of a ridge that the tile marked with the inward point, the place where the spiral stopped.

From the top of it the country opened, and Veera understood at last what she had been riding toward.

Below them the land fell into a basin that had once been prairie and was prairie no longer. It had been opened. Across a floor the size of a day’s ride the earth had been stripped to raw dirt and gouged everywhere with the parallel scars of the machine-wheels, and out of the dirt rose the oil-ravens.

Not fallen. Not the rusted broken teeth of the Plain of Memories, the drowned rigs her grandmother had called the world’s old punishment. These stood. Dozens of them, black iron towers slick with the thing they pulled up out of the ground, and at the foot of each one a flame burned at the lip of a pipe, a small ceaseless fire that gave the whole basin its light, so that the place glowed from below like a bed of coals under the yellow ceiling of its own smoke. Between the towers the machines moved, the large ones and the small, coming and going from a yard where Veera could see even at this distance the shapes of new machines being made, raised on scaffolds, fed from a lake.

A lake of it. The dead earth-blood, pooled and dammed behind earthworks, black and still and enormous, and the tanks drinking from the lake, and the machines drinking from the tanks, and the whole basin breathing its yellow breath up into a sky that no longer held any stars.

The hum was here too. It came up out of the basin through the ridge stone and into her knees, deeper than the convoy’s had been, a hum with a whole country under it.

Veera had carried her grandmother’s teaching all her life, that the oil-ravens had drilled too deep and broken the world into a single crowded landmass. She had believed it the way a person believes a thing that happened long before she was born. Now she looked down into a basin where someone had taught the fallen oil-ravens to stand back up, and she believed it the way a person believes a thing she is looking at.

Beside her, Orin made a sound that was not a word.

And then Veera saw the thing that turned the cold in her chest into something worse.

At the near edge of the basin, where the machine-yard met the stripped earth, stood a structure that did not belong to any of it. It was not petroleum-work. It was stone and timber, raised in a low circle, and its walls followed a geometry Veera had walked inside of only three days ago and would have known anywhere on earth. The sacred circle of an Honor Society compound. The same rings. The same proportions. Laid down here at the feet of the standing oil-ravens by hands that had learned the pattern in a forge far to the south.

“That is ours,” Orin said, very quietly. “That is our pattern. No one outside the Forgemasters is taught the whole of it.”

“One of them was,” Veera said.

She did not say the name. She did not need to. Orin had sat in the same council chamber she had, had seen the empty seventh chair, had heard Kessa say that the only living man who could read the oldest and most forbidden of the pattern-stones was the one who was no longer in his seat. The empty chair had ridden north with the two of them the whole way without either of them once speaking of it, and now it stood in the basin below, built in stone, holding up a corner of the thing that was eating the sky.

Above them the singing came again, nearer than it had been all day, and this time Veera looked up. She had decided that a rider who meant to ride down into that basin had earned the right to meet the eyes of whatever was counting her.

Three of them hung in the last of the light, wings held fixed and still, riding the heat that lifted off the burning basin. They were not searching now. They were watching. Watching her and Orin on the ridge, two riders where the spiral stopped, and Veera lifted one hand to them, not in surrender and not in threat, only in the way one rider acknowledged another across a distance too wide for words.

None of the three lifted a hand back. But none of them sang the killing pitch either. They hung a moment longer, adding whatever it was they added, and then peeled away to the north toward the high places, and were gone into the haze.

“Were they deciding?” Orin asked.

“They are always deciding.” Veera looked south, back down the long dark country they had crossed, toward a horizon the haze had swallowed. Somewhere past it were the compound and the wall and Sable, and the second attack everyone on that wall had known would come, the one the convoy would bring with everything it had learned. She could not see the smoke of it from here. She told herself that meant nothing. “So we give them something worth counting.”

They made no fire that night. They lay on the cold ridge above the burning basin with the two bikes between them, the ruined racing frame and the asymmetric one, and they did not sleep so much as wait. Below them the oil-ravens burned their small ceaseless flames through the dark, and the machines came and went without rest, and somewhere inside the stone circle that should not have existed, a man who had once held the seventh chair did whatever such a man did in the hours before the riders came down.

Veera held the carved horse in her hand until the dark began to thin.

Then she and Orin rose, and checked their frames, and started down toward the wells.

Chapter 12

The Reader’s Hand

The convoy came back at first light, and it came back changed.

Sable had spent the night on the eastern wall, because the eastern wall was where the first attack had broken and the eastern wall was where the second would come, and she wanted her eyes on the ground when it did. She had not slept. She had given up on sleep somewhere around the third hour, when the machines out in the outer fields had begun to move in the dark, not attacking, only repositioning, their engines idling low, their small ceaseless flames marking where each of them stood. All night they had rearranged themselves. By the time the light came up gray and wrong through the petroleum haze, Sable could read the new shape of them, and the new shape was worse.

The day before, the convoy had thrown itself at the eastern gate in one mass, the way a Scavenger band threw itself at a caravan, all weight and momentum and no patience. It had cost them. Today there was patience. The large machines had spread into a wide arc that reached from the eastern gate around toward the north wall, so that no single stretch could be reinforced from another without opening a gap somewhere else. The small two-wheeled scouts ranged in pairs along the arc, quick and constant, testing. And well back, ringed now by its own guard of machines, sat the fuel tank, the walking oil-raven, the thing that let them never tire. They had learned that the tank was the thing worth protecting. Someone had taught them that.

Sable knew who.

She had known since the council chamber two nights past, when the gene-reader’s disc had pulsed against his coat all evening at the same steady rate, neither faster nor slower, while everyone else in the room had been afraid. A disc read the body of whoever stood before it. That was the whole of its purpose. But a disc that pulsed on and on at one even rate while its wearer stood among frightened people was not reading anyone. It was doing something else.

She had said nothing. She had no proof, only a Scavenger’s distrust of a thing that stayed too even, and there had been a wall to hold.

* * *

The horn sounded. The arc began to move.

They came slower this time, and that was the terror of it. There was no headlong rush to meet with arrows and thrown lamps and the courtyard trap. There was a steady, grinding advance across the whole eastern face of the compound at once, the large machines rolling forward at a walking pace behind their armored flanks, the scouts darting ahead to draw fire and mark where the arrows fell thickest. Every place the defenders massed, the scouts noted, and the large machines simply went somewhere else. The wall had been built to turn attackers into a narrow killing-front. The machines refused to be narrowed.

Kessa moved along the parapet without hurry, and her lack of hurry was the only thing holding the younger riders in place. She sent Ulla to the north wall with a third of the archers when the arc reached for it. She sent Tibor’s people to shore the eastern gate where yesterday’s strike had broken a hinge. She kept the courtyard trap baited and quiet and did not spring it, because the machines had learned the trap and would not enter it, and a trap the enemy knew was worth more as a threat than as a spring.

For an hour the wall held by giving ground it could afford. Then a large machine reached the eastern gate and struck it, and the reinforced hinge that Tibor’s people had worked on all night held for two strikes and broke on the third, exactly as it had the day before, and the gate sagged inward, and the machine backed off to let a second come up behind it.

That was when Sable saw him.

He was not at his station. Kessa had placed the gene-reader on the inner wall with the wounded, where a gene-reader belonged, close to the bodies his instrument could read for the difference between a rider who would live and a rider who would not. But he was not with the wounded. Sable caught the pale shape of him at the base of the inner wall, moving without hurry, in the one direction no one else in the whole compound was moving. Toward the archive.

She did not shout. She did not raise an alarm that thirty people fighting for their lives would have heard as noise. She came down off the eastern wall and she followed him, and she loosened the tire-iron in its strap as she went.

* * *

The inner archive was the oldest building in the compound, a low windowless drum of fitted stone at the compound’s heart, and Marda was standing in its doorway with a lit torch in one hand and a key in the other.

Marda the Stone-Reader had spent fifty years among the pattern-stones. She had told the Council, two nights ago, that she would hold the archive with two-thirds of the trained crafters, and she had not said the rest of it: that if the wall fell, Marda meant to burn what was inside before any hand that should not touch it could. The oldest stones. The forbidden ones. The knowledge the Honor Society had agreed long ago to keep and never use, the same stones a seventh Forgemaster had read for two years before he rode north and did not come back.

The gene-reader stopped a few paces from her. His disc pulsed against his coat, steady, even, unafraid.

“Stand away from the door,” he said. “There is no reason for the stones to burn. In an hour this will be settled, and the stones will be wanted whole.”

“Wanted by whom,” Marda said. It was not a question.

“By those who are coming to set the measures right again.” He said it the way a man says a thing he has believed for a long time and has grown tired of not saying aloud. “You have all felt the world coming loose. A Worker who rides like a Forgemaster. A caste that cannot be read. Order held this world together after the Convergence. Measurement held it. Every rider in his place, every place in its blood. And now your Kessa hands a forge to a cargo-hauler because the wind blew the right way on a testing ground, and you call it courage. It is not courage. It is the first crack. Those who are coming will close it. They asked only for the stones, and for a wall left open at the right hour, and in return this compound would have been left standing under a measure that works.”

“You opened the wall for them,” Marda said. “You have been opening it since they crossed the ridges. Your little disc.”

“I read where the wall was weak, and I told them where to press. Yes.” He took a step toward her. “It was mercy. A short war is a small one. Give me the stones and there need not be a long one.”

Sable stepped out of the passage behind him.

“She is not going to give you the stones,” she said.

The gene-reader turned, and for the first time in two days his disc changed its rhythm. It jumped, once, and then raced, because now there was a body in front of it that it could not help but read, and the body was Sable’s, and what it read there did not reassure him.

“You are the Scavenger,” he said.

“I am the rider who does not trust a thing that stays too even.” Sable did not raise the tire-iron. She did not need to yet. “You measured everyone in that council chamber and you never once measured whether anyone was measuring you. That is the flaw in your whole caste. You believe the only knowing is the kind that shows on an instrument. You have spent your life certain that what cannot be read cannot be real.” She took a step. “And a Worker with no disc-reading and no training just rode past every scout you have into the country your masters came from, because her grandmother taught her a thing your instruments cannot find. You did not lose to Kessa’s sentiment. You lost to the part of us you decided did not count.”

He went for Marda then, because the door was the whole of the game and the Scavenger was only in the way of it. He was quick for a man who spent his life reading bodies rather than using his own. He was not quick enough. Sable had been quick her whole life, in the way a person is quick who grew up where slowness got you killed, and she put the tire-iron into the side of his knee and took him down before his hand reached the torch, and she kept him down.

Marda did not watch it. Marda turned, and set the torch to the oil-soaked kindling laid ready inside the door, and stepped back, and let fifty years of the oldest knowledge in the Honor Society begin to burn. The forbidden stones would not burn, being stone. But the pattern-leathers would, and the copied rubbings, and the indexes that told a reader which stone held which secret, and without the index the stones were only scratched rocks in a drum of smoke, unreadable in one lifetime. Whatever the gene-reader’s masters had sent their machines south to carry home, it was going up now into the same wrong sky as everything else.

The gene-reader made a sound Sable had not expected, a small broken sound, watching it go.

“You have unmade the measure,” he said.

“We were never as measured as you needed us to be,” Sable said. “That was always the part you got wrong.”

* * *

The wall held. It held the way a thing holds when it has decided that breaking is not permitted, which is to say it held and it bled.

The eastern gate went, in the end. The machines came through it into the courtyard the defenders had cleared and trapped, and the trap took two of them, and the fighting in the courtyard was the worst thing Sable had seen in twenty years of a life that had not been gentle. But Kessa had built the inner wall for exactly this hour, and the inner wall held, and by the time the sun was overhead somewhere above the haze the machines in the courtyard had stopped coming forward, because the ones behind them had stopped pushing.

They did not know why, on the wall. They only knew that the pressure eased. That the arc in the outer fields, which had been grinding forward all morning, paused, and held, and began, machine by machine, to pull back toward the tank.

Tibor was carried down from the eastern gate with a scout’s projectile in his shoulder and his Pattern-Keeper’s hands unhurt, which he noted aloud twice, as if the hands were the thing that mattered. Nine riders were dead. The compound had held, and everyone who had held it knew that it could not hold another morning like this one, and that whatever had made the machines pause was the only reason there might not have to be another morning like this one.

Sable brought the gene-reader up to the parapet, bound, so that Kessa could see him, and told her all of it in the flat few words the moment allowed. Kessa listened. She looked at the smoke rising from the archive, the compound’s own smoke, thin and bitter against the enemy’s yellow, and something moved across her face that was grief and was also something harder than grief.

“The seventh chair,” Kessa said. “And now the certifier’s. Two of the seven, rotted from inside, and I did not see either of them.” She looked north, past the walls, past the machines that were pulling back, into the haze where the country had no names. “She saw it. The Worker. Before any of us. She said the machines did not appear on the horizon by themselves, and she was standing in this compound less than a week.”

“She is out there now,” Sable said. “In the country they came from.”

“I know.” Kessa’s hands were flat on the parapet stone. “Something made them stop, Sable. Something in the north just made twelve machines that do not tire decide they had somewhere more urgent to be.”

Sable looked north with her. The machines in the outer fields had formed a column now, and the column was turning, and the walking oil-raven at the heart of it was turning with them, and the whole grinding weight of the thing that had come to end them was pointing itself back the way it had come.

“Then she is still riding,” Sable said.

She did not say the rest of it, which was that she had promised to ride north herself after three days, and that three days was tomorrow, and that whatever Veera had done in the country with no name, she had done it alone, the way the other rider Sable had loved had done the last thing she ever did, alone, because no one rode with her. She was not going to pay it twice.

She went to find a fresh bike, and to gather what riders could still ride, and to be ready to leave at first light for the north.

Chapter 13

What the Blood Kept

They came down into the basin in the gray hour before the sun, riding the switchbacks the spiral named, and the nearer they came to the standing oil-ravens the less the place looked like a thing people had built and the more it looked like a wound that had been kept from closing.

Up close the towers were vast. Each one rose on a lattice of black iron three times the height of a walking-house, and each one worked, a great counterweighted beam nodding up and down at its head, slow and patient and tireless, pulling the dead earth-blood up out of a ground that had held it since before the world broke the first time. The flame at each wellhead was not small up close. It was a standing pillar of fire the height of two riders, roaring where from the ridge it had seemed to flicker, and the heat of the whole field pushed against Veera’s face like a hand. The smell was no longer a smell. It was the air itself. Her enhanced lungs took it in and could make nothing of it, the way they had made nothing of it since the thorn country, reporting only that whatever this was, her body had never been built to breathe it. Somewhere on the switchbacks their own wheels’ songs had gone under, swallowed by the basin’s roar.

The operators did not see them come. There were fewer of them than Veera had feared, a few dozen at most, moving among the towers in the coverings that made them look, exactly as the Stone-Walkers had said, like ghosts, or like beings dug out of a deep ruin still wearing the strange skins of the world that ended. They did not walk like people who feared attack. They walked like people who feared the ground.

The operators were not guarding the wells. They were nursing them.

* * *

They left the bikes in the shadow of a fuel tank and went the last of the way on foot to the stone circle, and inside the circle they found the seventh Forgemaster.

Veera knew him for what he was before Orin said the name, because he was the only man in the basin not dressed as a ghost. Vance sat on a block of dressed Honor Society stone with his hands open on his knees, an old man in a Forgemaster’s leathers gone stiff with oil, and he watched them come through the gap in the circle with no surprise at all, as though he had been waiting a long time for two riders to arrive and had stopped believing they would.

“You built our pattern here,” Orin said. His voice shook. “You built the sacred circle at the foot of this.”

“I built a place to stand,” Vance said. His voice was quiet and ruined, a scholar’s voice worn down to gravel. “A man needs a place that is his own shape when everything around him is the wrong one. I told myself the geometry did not know what I was standing it beside.” He looked at his open hands. “The geometry always knows. That is the whole of what we teach. I forgot it, or I let myself forget it, which is worse, because forgetting is done to you and letting is done by you.”

“You gave them the stones,” Veera said.

“I read the stones. For two years I read the oldest ones, the ones we keep and swear never to use, and I told myself I was only preserving what would otherwise be lost.” His mouth twisted. “Then men came to me who could read the wanting in a scholar the way your gene-readers read blood. They did not ask for the stones. They asked me to explain them. Only to explain. What is the harm in explaining a thing that already exists. And the one who brought them to me wore a disc and certified my archive against the damp, and I trusted him, because he was of a caste built to be trusted with what cannot be seen.” He looked up. “Is he still in the compound?”

“He was,” Veera said. “He is stopped now.”

Something eased in the old man’s face, and something else broke.

“Then you should know the rest of it quickly, because we do not have long.” Vance stood, and it cost him. “They came from across the water. This is not the first world they have drunk. The Convergence did not break their lands the way it broke ours, and they have spent the years since building the strength to come and take a world that still has anything left in the ground. The machines are only the reaching hand. What they wanted from me was the deep-knowing, how the old ones drilled, how deep, how fast.” He gestured out at the nodding towers, and his hand shook. “They drilled deeper than the old ones ever dared. I told them the old ones fell because they went too deep. They heard it as instruction, not warning. They are down into the deep pressure now, the layer the old stones say the world keeps for itself, and it is coming up whether any of us wills it or not. The ground is going to break. It is only a question of whether it breaks for nothing, or whether it breaks and takes all of this with it.”

“You have been waiting,” Veera said. “For someone to help you make the breaking count.”

“I have been waiting to not do the last thing alone,” Vance said, and Veera thought of Sable on the wall, and did not say so.

* * *

The plan was not complicated. The complicated part had already been done, over months, by men drilling deeper than the world would allow. All that remained was to stop the ghosts from holding it back.

Vance knew the wellhead. He had helped them build the controls, the valves and the great counterweighted chokes that let the operators bleed off the deep pressure a little at a time and keep the blowout from coming. If those chokes were opened, all of them, at once, the pressure the ghosts had been carefully bleeding for weeks would come up in a single breath, and the ground would do what the ground had done once before to the whole world.

“The chokes are here, at the central head,” Vance said. “One man can open them if he does not care what happens to him after. The fire will find the fuel lake on its own once the head goes. You two need to be climbing the south switchback before the head goes, not after. There is no after, at the head.”

“Then we open them together and we all run,” Orin said.

“They will not all open from a run.” Vance almost smiled. “Some things you have to stay and hold. You are young. You have not learned yet how much of the world gets held by someone staying.” He looked at Veera, and there was a plea in it, and the plea was not for rescue. “Get her clear when it comes. She saw it before any of us. She should be the one who carries what it means back to a wall that will not believe her, again.”

The ghosts had seen them by then.

A cry went up across the field, thin through the coverings, and figures began to converge on the stone circle from among the towers, and the roar of a scout machine coughed and caught somewhere close. There was very little time, and then there was less.

* * *

Vance reached the central head and began to open the chokes, and the sound the ground made changed.

It was not loud, at first. It was a deepening. A note that came up through the soles of Veera’s boots and into the long bones of her legs, the way the machines’ rumble had come up through the ridge, but older than the machines, older than anything, a sound the earth had been holding under its breath for longer than there had been People to hear it. The nodding towers began to nod out of time with one another. A choke screamed as Vance hauled it open. The ghosts were running now, not toward the circle but away from it, because the ghosts knew that sound, and Veera did not know it, and it did not matter that she did not know it, because her body knew it, and her body had been waiting her whole life to be told.

The carved horse went hot in her pocket.

Not warm. Hot, in a way it had never been, the heat climbing into her palm faster than even her bred-wide heart should have sent it, and her hand was around it before she knew that she had reached. She had spent a lifetime learning not to argue with the warmth. She did not argue with it now.

Then the smell came, riding in on the heat the way it must always have been meant to, except that all her life she had been given only the warmth and never the thing the warmth was for. Under the petroleum, under the hot iron, under the burning that had swallowed every other smell in the basin, it came thin and sour and certain. Struck stone. Rot. Something below both, something from a depth the world kept for itself. She had never smelled it before in her life. She knew it the way she knew her own name. Her grandmother’s voice came up in her with it, from somewhere far below memory:

Some of the People smelled the world turning over before it turned. Those were the ones who ran. The running is the only reason there is anyone left to be told.

It was the smell of ground about to break, and her body had been keeping it for her, folded down through the blood, since long before the grandmother of her grandmother smelled it once and ran and lived.

She did not decide to move. Her body moved. She had Orin by the collar of his riding coat and was hauling him backward toward the south switchback before her mind had assembled a single reason, the same deep knowing that had turned a cargo transport into a war-horse on the Plain of Memories, that had carried her file to the right place on the tubing with her eyes shut. Orin fought her for half a step, still looking back at Vance, and then he felt whatever there was in her grip and he stopped fighting and he ran.

Behind them Vance opened the last choke. He did not run. He turned toward the head and put both his scholar’s hands flat against the stone of the circle he had built, his own shape in a place that was the wrong one, and he waited for the ground to take back what he had helped it lose.

The world broke.

It broke the way the old stones said it had broken the first time, in miniature, a single deep note becoming a roar becoming a thing past sound, and the central head went up in a column of fire and earth that Veera felt against her back like the flat of a giant’s hand, throwing her and Orin the last of the way up the switchback and down behind the lip of the ridge. The standing oil-ravens, the towers that had been taught to rise where their ancestors fell, fell. All of them. The ground opened under the basin and drew the whole operation down into itself, the towers and the tanks and the machine-yard and the lake of dead earth-blood, and the fire found the lake, and the basin that had glowed like a coal-bed became, for one endless breath, a second sun under the yellow sky.

Then the sky was not yellow anymore. The haze that had roofed the country since the thorn-grass, the smoke of a hundred wellheads, had nothing feeding it now, and it began, very slowly, to lift.

* * *

They lay behind the ridge for a long time while the basin burned itself hollow.

When Veera lifted her head, the first thing she saw was that she could see the sun. Not a dull coin behind haze. The actual sun, low and red through the last of the smoke, but whole, casting a shadow off the ridge stone that fell where a shadow should fall. She had not known how much she had missed a true shadow until she had one again.

The second thing she saw was the Gliders.

They came down out of the high places to the north in a long descending line, more of them than she had ever seen at once, twenty, thirty, their fixed wings catching the light off the burning basin, their resin cords silent. They did not sing the killing pitch. They did not sing at all. They swept low over the ridge where the two riders lay, and over the scattered ghosts who were fleeing north on foot toward masters who had just watched their reaching hand pulled into the ground, and the Gliders herded the ghosts the way a wind herds chaff, not killing, only counting.

One of them broke from the line and landed on the ridge, folding its great wing with a sound like a held breath let go, and stood there on the stone looking at Veera with the flat bright attention of a hawk deciding whether a thing was prey or kin.

Veera got to her feet. Her legs did not entirely agree to it. She stood anyway, because she had decided somewhere in the last hour that she was done meeting the sky lying down, and she looked back at the flyer, and she did not look away.

The Glider inclined its head. Once. The way one rider acknowledged another across a distance too wide for words.

Then it opened its wing and was gone into the light, and Veera stood on the ridge with the sun on her face and the carved horse cooling slowly in her hand, and below her the basin burned down toward the ruin of the wells, and somewhere far to the south a compound she could not see was watching twelve machines run dry.

Chapter 14

The Wind at Her Back

The haze was gone by the time they cleared the thorn country, and the sky it left behind was the first honest blue Veera had seen since she rode out through the eastern gate.

It went slowly. The smoke of a hundred wellheads did not lift in an hour. But it lifted, thinning from a ceiling into a smear and from a smear into a few high torn clouds, and by the second morning of the ride south the sun came up clean over the bone-grass and threw the long true shadows of two riders across ground that had begun, here and there, to remember how to be green. The crystals were gone from the dry watercourses. A bird crossed the sky, low and ordinary and going somewhere, and Orin laughed at it, once, a cracked and disbelieving sound.

The racing bike did not make it out of the thorn country. It had given everything a frame could give on the ride north, and somewhere in the gray trees the rear wheel that Orin had trued around a missing spoke finally folded, and the beautiful ruined self-portrait lay down in the dust for the last time. Orin knelt over it for a while. Then he did what an Honor Society rider does with a bike that has died, which is to take from it the one piece that will ride into the next frame, and he broke the head-badge free with his knife and put it in the pocket over his ribs beside the Scavenger leather, and he stood up.

“We are not making it,” Veera said, “we are continuing it.” It was the Forgemasters’ own phrase, and she had heard it only once, from Kessa.

They rode double after that, on the asymmetric frame Veera had built in the trial, the strange unbalanced bike that the Council had argued over. It carried two riders across a country of broken ground and did not complain. Two tired riders were only another kind of shifting load. Orin rode behind her with his hands at her waist and did not speak of it, and Veera did not speak of it, and the wind was at their backs the whole way.

* * *

The Stone-Walker waystation still stood, and the woman with the shorter stilts was still there, and she was not alone.

A Glider stood beside her on the platform. Its wing was folded down its back, and up close it was smaller than Veera had imagined a flying thing could be, a lean weathered person no taller than Orin, wrapped in colors that had been chosen to be seen against sky and never against ground. It watched the two riders come in on one bike with the flat bright attention Veera had met on the ridge above the burning basin.

“The high places counted you,” the Glider said, without greeting, as though the conversation had been going on for some time and Veera had only just come within earshot. “You rode down into the thing that could not be beaten, and you rode back out, and behind you it fell. We have not seen a rider earn the count in a long season. We came to tell you what the counting buys.”

Veera got down off the bike. Her legs held her this time.

“Tell me,” she said.

“You are not the first nose to smell the world turn over.” The Glider said it plainly. “Your grandmothers were not the only ones who ran when the deep places broke. There were others, in other lands, who smelled it and ran, and some of them ran to the high places, and the high places have kept what they carried ever since, because what is kept in the high air does not rot and is not measured and cannot be bred out. We know what your blood is, Veera-Who-Rides-Steel-Horses. We have known longer than your gene-readers have known how to draw a disc.”

“Then you know what came for us,” Veera said. “You know where the machines came from.”

“From across the water.” The Glider’s flat eyes did not blink. “This land broke and crowded into one country when the world came together. Other lands did not break the same way. They kept their coasts, and their cities, and their hunger, and they have spent the long years building strength while we have spent them building bicycles. What you burned in the basin was a hand. The body is across the sea, and it has other hands.” The Glider let that stand a moment in the clean air. “You have made an enemy that does not yet know your name. It will learn it. When it does, you will need what the high places keep. Come to us, when you have carried your people through the winter. Climb to where the air is thin and we will teach you the rest of what your blood already half-remembers. There is more of this story, rider. You hold one true piece of it. We hold others.”

Then it opened its wing and stepped off the platform into a wind Veera could not feel, and rose, and was a shape against the blue, and then was less than a shape, and then was gone north toward the peaks.

The Stone-Walker woman watched it go. “They have not spoken that many words to a ground-rider in my lifetime,” she said. “You should feel the weight of it.”

“I do,” Veera said. She felt it the way she felt the carved horse, a thing that did not need to be looked at to be known.

* * *

Sable met them a half day north of the compound, riding hard the wrong way.

She had ridden out at first light, exactly three days after the eastern ridge, with a handful of riders behind her and a promise she had made to herself twenty years too late, and when she crested a rise and saw two figures on one bike coming south out of the clean country, she stopped so suddenly that the rider behind her nearly went into her wheel. She did not shout. Sable did not shout. But she sat on her bike in the middle of the plain with her flat unreadable face doing something that was not flat and was not unreadable at all, and she waited for them to reach her, because her legs, for once, did not entirely trust themselves.

“You rode north,” Veera said, when she was close enough.

“I said I would. After three days.” Sable looked at the two of them on the one frame, at Orin alive behind her, at the ruin of the country behind them both that was already beginning to green. “You did not make me finish the ride. That is the first time.” She did not explain what it was the first time of. She did not have to.

“The wall?” Orin asked.

“Held. Barely. Cost us nine, and the archive, and it cost the gene-reader everything he was, which I do not grieve.” Sable turned her bike to ride south with them. “The machines ran dry two mornings ago. Stopped where they stood in the outer fields and would not start again, and the ghosts inside them climbed out and started walking north on foot, which they will not enjoy.” She glanced at Veera. “We did not know why. Kessa said something in the north had made twelve machines that never tire decide they had somewhere to be. I told her it was you. She said she already knew.”

* * *

The compound was standing.

It was scarred in ways it had not been a week ago, the eastern gate a black ruin, the courtyard behind it a graveyard of stopped machines, the inner archive a cold heap of ash that Marda would not let anyone clear yet. But its banners flew, and its forge-smoke rose thin and ordinary into a sky that was blue enough now to show it, and when Veera rode through the broken eastern gate on the bike she had built, with Orin behind her and Sable at her side, the compound stopped what it was doing to watch her come.

Not the way they had watched her come the first time, a Worker with a damaged transport and a lucky story. They watched her the way the Honor Society watched a rider who had earned the watching. Kessa was in the courtyard, gray and burned and unbent. Ulla stood beside her, and when Veera brought the bike to a stop, it was Ulla who spoke first, Ulla who had fought her admission at every step.

“There is no name for what you are,” Ulla said. “You are not a Worker. You are not a Forgemaster. The blood-registers do not keep a line for a rider who went down into that and came back out. I spent most of my life certain that a person who did not fit the lines was a danger to them.” She looked at the bike, at the two riders it had carried home. “I was wrong about the danger. I was not wrong that you do not fit. So we will stop trying to make you fit. We will give you the work instead. The roads no one else can read. The rides no one else comes back from. That is not a title. It is only a job, and it is yours if you will have it.”

“Vance is dead,” Veera said. “He opened the wells and stayed to hold them open. He built our circle at the foot of the worst thing I have ever seen, and then he used the last of himself to pull it into the ground. I do not know what to call a man like that. I do not think there is one word for him.”

“There is not,” Kessa said. “The seventh chair, and the certifier’s. Two of the seven rotted from the inside, and I did not see either of them go.” She did not offer Veera a chair, and Veera understood that too, and was grateful for it. “We will leave both empty a while. Not for their shame. For the size of the questions they leave behind.” She looked at the bike Veera had ridden in on, the asymmetric frame that had carried two, and something in her stern burned face eased. “You built a work-horse that is also a war-horse, and it carried a second rider home. I told you, on the day you arrived, that fall is how we learn to fly.” She put a scarred hand on the crossbar. “Welcome home, rider.”

* * *

That night the compound buried its nine and mourned its archive and did not pretend it had won more than it had won.

Veera sat on the western wall, where Sable had stood the night she watched Orin ride north, and she looked out at the plain in the last of a clean sunset, and Orin came and sat beside her, and for a while neither of them said anything, which was a thing they had both become good at.

“The Glider said the body is across the water,” Veera said finally. “That we burned a hand. That it will learn my name.”

“Then we have work,” Orin said.

“We have work.” She turned the carved horse over in her fingers, the small wooden thing a Stone-Walker had dropped at her feet on a day when she was only a Worker who had gotten lucky, the thing that had warmed in her hand every time her blood knew something before she did, all the way back to the beginning, though she had not understood it then. She understood it now. Not all of it. The Glider had said she half-remembered, and half was the honest word for it. But she knew what the warmth was for, at last, and knowing it did not make it smaller. It made the horizon larger.

Far to the north, past the greening plain, past the ruin of the basin, the high places caught the last light and held it after the plain had let it go. Veera looked at the peaks a long time. Somewhere up there, in air too thin to breathe and too high to measure, people who flew were keeping the other half of what her blood remembered, and they had told her to come and learn it when her people were through the winter.

Her people would get through the winter. She would see to that first.

And then she would climb.

The wind came up out of the south, warm and clean and smelling of nothing but grass, and it filled the torn banners along the wall, and it moved through the compound the way it had once moved through a cargo sail on the Plain of Memories, on a morning when a Worker first learned that the grinding work of hauling could become, with the right wind and the right hands, something close to flight.

Veera closed her hand around the horse, and let the wind take the last of the smoke out of the sky, and watched the high places hold the light.

THE END OF BOOK ZERO

Appendix

A Reader’s Codex of the One Land

The lore-keepers of the bicycle-civilizations do not agree on much, but they agree on this: a people that forgets how the world broke will break it again. What follows is a traveler’s codex, gathered from the pattern-stones of the Honor Society, the wind-songs of the Stone-Walkers, the pattern-leathers of the Scavengers, and the long memory of the Worker grandmothers. It is offered here in the spirit the grandmothers offered it to their children. Not as a map, because the country keeps changing, but as a way of seeing.

The Breaking of the World

Once there were many lands, divided by oceans no rider could cross. Then the drilling went too deep. In their hunger for the dead earth-blood beneath the bedrock, the people of the old world bored past the layer the earth keeps for itself, and the earth answered the only way it could. It folded. Coastlines were driven together, oceans were swallowed, and the scattered continents were crushed into a single wounded landmass. The lore-keepers call this the Great Convergence.

The dead rigs that caused it still stand in places, rusted the color of dried blood, and the Stone-Walkers named them oil-ravens. Where an oil-raven fell, the ground at its feet is buckled and cracked, as though the earth had tried to spit it out. Ride past enough of them and you learn the history without being told it, which is how the grandmothers preferred to teach.

What ended in the Convergence was not human knowledge. It was human forgetting. The tools that had served the body for ten thousand years before the burning, the lever and the wheel and the patient mathematics of the gear, went on working, because their power came from muscle and wind and water rather than from the thing under the ground. The people who survived were, more often than not, the people who had never stopped riding.

The Three Knowings

Knowledge moves through the surviving peoples in three ways, and every lore-keeper is a custodian of one of them.

Hand-knowledge passes from master to apprentice through the body. A young Forgemaster does not learn to braze a frame from a book. She learns by standing at an elder’s shoulder for years, until her wrists know the heat of a joint before her eyes have read it. Hand-knowledge is the slowest to teach and the hardest to lose. It cannot be stolen. It can only be earned.

Voice-knowledge travels in song. The wind-singers of the Stone-Walkers carry whole catalogs of tool dimensions in metered verse, each measurement fixed in the count of its syllables so that it survives the rough weather of a nomadic life. A forge-elder can sing the exact taper of two hundred harpoon-points, and the rhythm of the song is the rhythm of the work.

Mark-knowledge lives in inscribed surfaces: the pattern-stones of the Honor Society, the pattern-leathers of the Scavengers, the shell-plaques of the Coastal Nation. It is portable, durable, and indifferent to who reads it, which is its gift and its danger both. A pattern can outlast the people who swore to keep it. A pattern can be read by a hand it was never meant for. Much in this story turns on that second truth.

The Peoples of the One Land

The Honor Society keeps the forges. They live in circular compounds raised behind earthwork walls, governed by a council of Forgemasters, and they hold that a rider and a machine should become a single trained instrument. They prize the finished thing, the frame brought to tolerance, the joint that will never fail. Their virtue is discipline. Their flaw is a quiet certainty that anything worth having can be measured.

The Stone-Walkers cross the poisoned lowlands on jointed steel stilts and live in walking-houses that stride above the salt on articulated legs. They are nomads and watchers, and they have kept their eyes on the north longer than anyone. It was a Stone-Walker who first warned Veera that the petroleum dragons had begun to dream of waking, and Stone-Walker bows that first bought the compound its time.

The Scavengers build from what the broken world left behind, and they hold that a machine is never finished, only continued. Where the Honor Society reveres the completed frame, the Scavenger sees an argument not yet won. Their bikes are mismatched, patched, perpetually rebuilt, and still rolling long after prettier machines have cracked and been left in the grass. To cross over from the Scavengers into any other people is to leave a whole way of seeing behind, and those who do it carry the leaving with them for the rest of their lives.

The Coastal Nation lives in the mangrove canopies, breathing the thick salt air with lungs bred deep for it, building bicycles of bamboo and resin-laminated cane that weigh a fraction of any steel frame and glow at night with cultured light. They read the tides the way the prairie peoples read the wind.

The Thrill Seekers live among the coastal pines and ride for the joy of riding. They tune their machines for sensation and reconfigure them in an afternoon, racer to acrobat to long-hauler, and they keep their family trees with as much care as other peoples keep their tools.

The Gliders launch from the peaks on wings of birdbone and treated cloth, and no one on the ground is entirely certain whose side they are on. They watch. They count. And in the thin air where nothing rots and nothing can be weighed on any instrument, they keep something the lowland peoples lost. What that something is, and what their counting buys a rider who earns it, the high places have only begun to say.

And in the north, the machines. Of them, more below, though the honest truth is that less is known than feared.

The Reading of Blood

At birth, every child of the settled peoples is measured by a gene-reader, whose disc reads heart-strength and lung-capacity and the other attributes the body can be made to confess. From that reading a caste is assigned, and the caste is for life. Workers are bred broad-chested and deep-lunged for the hauling of cargo, with pale streaks marking the shoulders and collarbones so that the blood shows plainly on the body. Scouts are bred lean and quick-eyed for reconnaissance. Supervisors are gene-blessed into a managerial class that wears chrome monitors, rides decorative bikes, and does not, by design, need to work. The Honor Society earns its ranks instead by public examination, apprentice to journeyman to master to Forgemaster, but even the Honor Society keeps gene-readers to certify the blood.

The gene-readers’ instruments are precise, and their measurements are true as far as they reach. What the discs cannot find is the thing the Worker grandmothers call blood-memory: knowledge that lives in the body and the reflex, inherited from ancestors who once rode Sacred Horses across plains that no longer exist. A hand that knows where the file should fall before the eye has read the joint. A body that turns a cargo transport into a war-horse without ever being taught how. A warmth in a carved thing that climbs into the palm a breath before the mind understands why.

The gene-readers deny that blood-memory is real, and they must, because their whole authority rests on the claim that a person is the sum of what can be measured, and blood-memory is the part of a person that no disc has ever read. The Stone-Walkers say the salvaged steel remembers where it came from. The grandmothers say the wheels sing the old songs. The gene-readers say these are only stories. The grandmothers do not argue. They go on remembering, and they teach their children to do the same, and now and then the world turns on a thing that no instrument in it could measure.

Steel Horses: A Field Guide to the Bicycles

A vehicle in the One Land is not merely transport. It is identity, the way a horse once revealed its rider by the wear on the saddle and the songs the wheels sing when they turn.

The cargo transport is the Worker’s mount, and the rarest of all bikes to the outsider’s eye, because the caste that rides it is invisible to those who reckon worth in chrome. It is also the largest and, in its way, the most cunningly built. It stands taller than its rider when loaded, forged of heavy tubing reinforced at every stress point, and it carries a great triangular sail of grass-cloth on a mast at its center, angled by a rudder lever at the rider’s hand. With the wind behind it the sail does the work; across the wind, the sail is set to turn the gust into forward thrust, the same principle that once drove the sailing ships now rotting on the coasts. Its wheels are spoked to hum when they turn, and the hum is not decoration. A tuned wheel reports its own health in its song, and a rider who listens can hear a bent spoke long before she could see it.

The supervisor bike is jewelry, not a working machine, slender and silk-sailed and dyed to announce a rank. It is too light to haul, too fragile to fight, and its only true cargo is the message that its rider does not need to work.

The Honor Society builds three bikes of its own. The war-bike is heavy and low-geared and built to give and take violence, double-spoked so a broken spoke will not fold the wheel, hung with mounts for blades and harpoons, and ridden in formation rather than alone. The racing bike is the most beautiful thing the Honor Society makes, brazed of the lightest tubing the Forgemasters dare, its spokes trued to acoustic silence so it runs without a song. And the ceremonial bike is the one an initiate builds for themselves at their Rite of Passage, a self-portrait in steel meant to be ridden for life. When its rider dies, its metal is most often melted into the next initiate’s frame, so that every active bike carries atoms of every Forgemaster who came before. We are not making this bike, the Forgemasters say. We are continuing it.

The Scavenger bike is whatever its rider can build from what they find, mismatched and welded and rebuilt until the joints show repair upon repair. It will lose a race to an Honor Society frame and outlive it by a decade. Beyond these, the Coastal Nation builds flexing composite frames that glow with cultured light; the Thrill Seekers build sprung, reconfigurable machines tuned for sensation; and a rare few compounds have begun to build the glider-hybrid, a bicycle with a folding wing that lets a rider launch from a ridge and glide, a machine whose tactics no one has fully worked out yet.

Conveyances Beyond the Wheel

Not everything in the One Land rolls. The Stone-Walker stilt is a geared steel leg, eight to twelve feet tall, that amplifies a walker’s stride until they cross the ground at the speed of a galloping horse, with a harpoon-mount built in at the hand. The walking-house is dwelling, fortress, and vehicle at once, a multi-story structure on four to six articulated legs, powered from within by inhabitants treading a great drum, lifting its people above the salt-poisoned earth and the storms that sweep the lowlands. And the glider apparatus is a wing of birdbone and treated cloth from which a flier hangs in a harness, steering by weight and by cords, launched from peaks and from the tall stone launching-towers. In flight it is silent, until its rider chooses otherwise. The Glider warriors set whistles and howling cords into their wing-frames, and a formation descending in full cry is a sound the lowland peoples have learned to fear before they have learned to see it.

The Petroleum Dragons

Of the northern machines, the codex can offer only what the Stone-Walkers have seen, told in words that may be plain or may be metaphor, or may be both at once.

They walk but do not breathe. Their legs and wheels are driven by no rider’s muscle, and they move for hours without rest, day and night, without showing fatigue. They smell of fire, venting the smoke of burning oil, the same dead earth-blood whose drilling ended the old world. They grind as they come, a rolling mechanical noise heard from miles off that scatters birds and terrifies animals and announces them long before they can be seen. They carry weapons that throw fast projectiles by explosive charge, further and harder than any harpoon. And they are crewed by human operators who dress in coverings against their own machines’ smoke, so that the Stone-Walkers say they look like ghosts, or like the pre-Convergence dead found in deep ruins still wearing the strange garments of the world that ended.

What the machines want is no longer entirely a mystery. Veera rode to the wells and back and learned the shape of it, and closed one reaching hand of the thing. But a hand is not a body. The body, the high places say, is across the water, in lands the Convergence did not break the way it broke this one, and it has other hands, and it does not yet know her name. It will. That, however, is a matter for the next book.

A Glossary of the One Land

Blood-memory. Knowledge that lives in the body and the reflex, inherited from ancestors who rode the Sacred Horses. No gene-reader’s disc has ever detected it. Its workings are never fully explained, only felt.

The counting. What the Gliders do above every rider, walker, and machine that passes beneath them. What the counting buys, the high places have only begun to say.

Dead earth-blood. Petroleum, in the speech of the grandmothers. Spoken of rarely, and in a low voice.

Gene-readers. The caste that measures. Their discs weigh heart-strength and lung-capacity at birth, and from the weighing a life is assigned. What the discs cannot find, the readers hold does not exist.

The Great Convergence. The drilling catastrophe that ended the petroleum age. The old world bored past the layer the earth keeps for itself, and the earth answered by folding, crushing the scattered continents into the One Land.

Oil-ravens. The drilling rigs of the old world. Where one fell, the ground at its feet is buckled and salted, and nothing grows in the ring. In the north, some were taught to stand again.

Oil-raven steel. High steel salvaged from the fallen rigs, prized by the Stone-Walkers, who temper it with prayers and hold that the metal remembers where it came from.

Petroleum dragons. The dormant oil and the machines that might wake it. For three generations, a story to frighten children. No longer.

The Plain of Memories. The prairie heartland, cut by paths worn deeper than weather can fill, older than oil.

The Rite of Passage. The Honor Society’s initiation, in which an initiate builds a ceremonial bike as a self-portrait in steel and rides it for life.

Sacred Horses. The beasts the People rode before the world broke. Extinct, the readers say. The grandmothers do not argue.

Star-roads and wind-paths. The old navigation, the patterns of the night sky and of the prevailing winds, taught mouth to ear.

The Three Knowings. How knowledge survives: in the hand, passed master to apprentice; in the voice, carried by song; in the mark, cut into stone, leather, and shell.

Tlalli-tetl. Refined plant oil, the lamp-fuel and lubricant of the settled lands. In the Worker tongue, sacred earth-blood. In a Supervisor’s ledger, plant oil, grade one.

The Trial of Understanding. Three days, a bare frame, and the forges. The Honor Society’s test of whether a builder understands the partnership between rider and mount. It does not grade on proximity to success.
